### Phases and Activities

The project lifecycle will consist of five distinct phases, moving from initial concept to final closure, ensuring structured progression and control.

#### I. Project Initiation & Definition
This phase establishes the foundational elements of the project, ensuring alignment with organizational objectives and setting the stage for detailed planning.
i.  **Identify and Define Project Objectives:** Clarify the specific research questions, expected outcomes, and value proposition. A project is a "temporary endeavour to achieve one or more defined objectives" [Ref 5, 15].
ii. **Identify and Engage Project Sponsor:** Secure commitment from the "person responsible for obtaining the resources and executive decisions to enable success" [Ref 5].
iii. **Identify and Analyze Stakeholders:** Systematically identify all "person, group or organization that has interests in, or can affect, be affected by" the project [Ref 5]. Develop a stakeholder engagement plan to "effectively engage with stakeholders" [Ref 9].
iv. **Develop High-Level Project Scope and Deliverables:** Define the overall boundaries and key outputs of the research project. Deliverables are "unique and verifiable element[s] that are required to be produced by a project" [Ref 11].
v.  **Establish Project Governance Framework:** Define the "principles, policies and procedures by which a project is authorized and directed to accomplish agreed objectives" [Ref 8]. This includes the structure of decision-making bodies.
vi. **Create Project Charter:** Document the project's purpose, objectives, high-level requirements, assumptions, constraints, and initial budget/schedule, formally authorizing the project.

#### II. Detailed Planning
This phase elaborates on the project charter, producing a comprehensive Project Management Plan that guides all subsequent activities.
i.  **Develop Project Management Plan (PMP):** Compile all subsidiary plans into a single, cohesive document guiding "coordinated activities to direct and control the accomplishment of agreed objectives" [Ref 5]. This encompasses elements like "Product scope, Project scope, Estimating, Scheduling, Budget, Team composition, Communication plan, Procurement, Change management, Measurement" [Ref 6].
ii. **Define Scope and Develop Work Breakdown Structure (WBS):** Decompose the defined scope into progressively lower levels of "elements of work" and "work packages" [Ref 4]. This clarifies "what the project will deliver" [Ref 3].
iii. **Develop Schedule and Budget:** Estimate activity durations, sequence activities, and develop the project schedule and cost baseline [Ref 6].
iv. **Plan for Quality Management:** Define processes and standards to "build quality into processes and deliverables" specific to education research [Ref 9].
v.  **Plan for Resource Management:** Determine and acquire the necessary team members, equipment, and materials, including "Team composition" [Ref 6].
vi. **Plan for Risk Management:** Identify potential "threat risk occurrence[s] that would have a negative impact" [Ref 4, 12], assess their impact and likelihood, and plan "risk responses" [Ref 9]. Also identify opportunities.
vii. **Plan for Communication and Stakeholder Engagement:** Detail how project information will be shared with stakeholders and how their needs will be addressed [Ref 6, 9].
viii. **Plan for Procurement (if applicable):** Outline how external goods and services will be acquired [Ref 6].
ix. **Tailor the Approach:** "Tailor based on context" [Ref 9] considering the research project's specific needs, applying PRINCE2 principles that are "fit for the project context" [Ref 10].

#### III. Execution & Research
This phase involves performing the planned work to achieve the project objectives and create deliverables.
i.  **Direct and Manage Project Work:** Lead the project team to execute activities defined in the PMP [Ref 2].
ii. **Perform Research Activities:** Conduct data collection, analysis, experimentation, and other core research tasks as per the research methodology.
iii. **Acquire and Develop Project Team:** Secure and manage the project team, fostering a "collaborative project team environment" [Ref 9].
iv. **Manage Communications:** Distribute information to stakeholders according to the communication plan.
v.  **Engage Stakeholders:** Continuously interact with stakeholders, managing their expectations and addressing their concerns [Ref 9].
vi. **Conduct Procurements:** Administer procurement contracts for external goods and services [Ref 6].
vii. **Implement Risk Responses:** Execute planned actions to address identified risks and opportunities [Ref 9].
viii. **Develop Deliverables:** Produce tangible or intangible "elements that are required to be produced by a project" [Ref 11], such as research reports, methodologies, or educational materials.

#### IV. Monitoring & Controlling
This phase involves tracking, reviewing, and regulating the progress and performance of the project, identifying areas where the plan may require changes.
i.  **Monitor Project Work and Performance:** Track actual progress against the baselines for scope, schedule, and cost. This involves "Measurement" [Ref 2, 6].
ii. **Control Scope:** Manage changes to the project scope and ensure that all work performed is within the agreed boundaries.
iii. **Control Schedule:** Monitor schedule performance, identify variances, and take corrective actions.
iv. **Control Costs:** Monitor cost performance, identify variances, and manage the project budget.
v.  **Perform Quality Control:** Monitor specific project results to determine if they comply with relevant quality standards and identify ways to eliminate causes of unsatisfactory performance.
vi. **Manage Change Control:** Utilize a formal "Change management" process to review, approve, and manage changes to deliverables, organizational assets, and the project management plan [Ref 6].
vii. **Monitor and Control Risks:** Continuously monitor for new risks and re-evaluate existing ones, adjusting risk responses as needed [Ref 9].
viii. **Report Performance:** Communicate project status, progress, and forecasts to stakeholders.
ix. **Manage Issues:** Address "event[s] that arise dur[ing] project" execution that require attention [Ref 11].

#### V. Project Closure
This final phase formally completes the project, ensuring all administrative activities are performed and lessons learned are documented.
i.  **Finalize All Activities:** Ensure all work defined in the scope has been completed.
ii. **Obtain Formal Acceptance of Deliverables:** Secure sign-off from the project sponsor and key stakeholders that the "deliverable[s]" are acceptable [Ref 11].
iii. **Perform Administrative Closure:** Complete all contracts, update records, and formally close out the project.
iv. **Release Project Resources:** Formally release project team members and other resources.
v.  **Document Lessons Learned:** Capture knowledge gained throughout the project lifecycle to improve future projects.
vi. **Prepare Final Project Report:** Summarize project performance, outcomes, and stakeholder satisfaction.
vii. **Project Archiving:** Archive all relevant project documents and data.

### Roles

Key roles are essential for clear accountability and effective project execution.
*   **Project Sponsor:** "Person responsible for obtaining the resources and executive decisions to enable success" [Ref 5]. Provides high-level direction and oversight.
*   **Project Manager:** "Coordinated activities to direct and control the accomplishment of agreed objectives" [Ref 5]. Responsible for overall project planning, execution, monitoring, and control [Ref 13, 14].
*   **Research Lead / Principal Investigator (PI):** Directs the scientific and academic aspects of the research, ensuring methodological rigor and quality of research outcomes.
*   **Project Steering Committee / Governance Board:** Provides "project governance" and strategic guidance, authorizing and directing the project [Ref 8].
*   **Project Team Members / Researchers:** Individuals responsible for executing specific research tasks and activities, contributing to deliverables [Ref 9, 14].
*   **Quality Assurance Lead:** Ensures adherence to research quality standards and ISO requirements for processes and deliverables [Ref 9].
*   **Key Stakeholders:** "Person, group or organization that has interests in, or can affect, be affected by" the project [Ref 5]. Includes beneficiaries, regulatory bodies, and internal departments.

### Decision Gates

Decision gates are formal points within the project lifecycle where critical decisions are made, often leading to project continuation, modification, or termination. These ensure proper "project governance" [Ref 8].

1.  **Gate 1: Project Charter Approval (End of Phase I)**
    *   **Purpose:** To formally authorize the project and commit necessary resources.
    *   **Decision Makers:** Project Sponsor, Steering Committee [Ref 8].
    *   **Inputs:** Approved Project Charter, high-level scope, initial business case.
    *   **Outcome:** Go/No-Go decision to proceed to detailed planning.

2.  **Gate 2: Project Management Plan Approval (End of Phase II)**
    *   **Purpose:** To approve the comprehensive plan for how the project will be executed, monitored, and controlled.
    *   **Decision Makers:** Project Sponsor, Steering Committee [Ref 8].
    *   **Inputs:** Approved Project Management Plan (including detailed scope, schedule, budget, risk plan) [Ref 6].
    *   **Outcome:** Go/No-Go decision to proceed with project execution.

3.  **Gate 3: Mid-Project Review / Phase Completion (During Phase III)**
    *   **Purpose:** To assess progress, review interim research findings, manage any significant changes, and re-confirm continued viability for the remainder of the project.
    *   **Decision Makers:** Project Sponsor, Steering Committee, Project Manager.
    *   **Inputs:** Performance reports, risk updates, interim deliverables, change requests.
    *   **Outcome:** Approval to continue execution, with potential adjustments to the plan.

4.  **Gate 4: Final Deliverable Acceptance (End of Phase IV)**
    *   **Purpose:** To obtain formal acceptance from the sponsor and key stakeholders that the primary research outcomes and deliverables meet the defined objectives and quality standards.
    *   **Decision Makers:** Project Sponsor, Key Stakeholders.
    *   **Inputs:** Completed and validated deliverables, quality reports [Ref 11].
    *   **Outcome:** Formal acceptance of deliverables, allowing progression to project closure.

5.  **Gate 5: Project Close-Out Approval (End of Phase V)**
    *   **Purpose:** To formally conclude the project, acknowledge its completion, and approve the close-out documentation.
    *   **Decision Makers:** Project Sponsor, Steering Committee.
    *   **Inputs:** Project closure report, lessons learned documentation, financial closure documentation.
    *   **Outcome:** Formal project closure.
