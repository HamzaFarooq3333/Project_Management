### Process Summary

This process recommendation outlines a streamlined, yet robust, approach for managing a small (< 6 months), information technology research project using the PRINCE2 methodology. PRINCE2, as a process-based approach, provides a structured framework for project management, emphasizing clear responsibilities and control throughout the project lifecycle [Ref 3, Ref 7]. Given the research nature and IT industry, the process incorporates elements of iterative development within PRINCE2's stage-based structure, allowing for flexibility inherent in research while maintaining governance. PMBOK guidance on planning, scope, and team composition [Ref 1] is integrated into PRINCE2's initiation and controlling stages, ensuring comprehensive coverage. ISO21502 provides overarching guidance on project management applicability across various project types and complexities [Ref 2, Ref 10], reinforcing the adaptability of this framework. Key elements include defining clear objectives, managing risks (threats [Ref 5, Ref 13]), planning for research deliverables, and maintaining stakeholder engagement [Ref 4, Ref 6].

### Phases and Activities

The process is structured around the seven PRINCE2 processes, adapted for a small, short-duration research project.

#### I. Starting Up a Project (SU)
This phase focuses on ensuring there's a sound basis for the project before significant resources are committed.
i. Appoint the Executive and Project Manager.
ii. Capture previous lessons learned from similar research or IT projects.
iii. Design and appoint the project management team, defining roles and responsibilities [Ref 3].
iv. Prepare the Outline Business Case (justifying the research need and potential value).
v. Select the project approach (e.g., iterative research, prototyping).
vi. Assemble the Project Brief (initial project definition, research objectives, scope, and expected outcomes).
vii. Plan the Initiation Stage.

#### II. Directing a Project (DP)
This process runs throughout the project, enabling the Project Board to make key decisions and provide overall direction.
i. Authorize initiation of the project [Ref 12].
ii. Authorize the project itself (approval of the Project Initiation Documentation).
iii. Authorize a stage or exception plan.
iv. Give ad hoc direction as needed, addressing issues and risks [Ref 11].
v. Authorize project closure.

#### III. Initiating a Project (IP)
This phase establishes a solid foundation for the project by detailing planning and control mechanisms.
i. Agree on product scope and project scope, including specific research questions and expected deliverables [Ref 1, Ref 5].
ii. Plan quality for research deliverables (e.g., peer review, data validation).
iii. Plan the project by refining the initial research objectives, estimating timeframes and resources (< 6 months constraint), and detailing the team composition [Ref 1].
iv. Refine the Business Case (detailing expected benefits from research findings).
v. Set up project controls (monitoring progress, managing change [Ref 1]).
vi. Create the Project Initiation Documentation (PID), covering research objectives, scope, roles, plan, quality, risks, and communication [Ref 1].
vii. Assemble Risk Management Strategy, focusing on research-specific uncertainties and threats [Ref 5, Ref 13].
viii. Create Communication Management Strategy, outlining how stakeholders will be engaged and informed [Ref 1, Ref 4].

#### IV. Controlling a Stage (CS)
This process describes the day-to-day management of a stage by the Project Manager.
i. Authorize work package (specific research tasks or experiments).
ii. Review work package status and progress against the research plan.
iii. Capture and examine project issues and research-specific problems [Ref 11].
iv. Escalate issues and risks to the Project Board when necessary.
v. Take corrective action to keep the stage within tolerance.
vi. Report stage progress to the Project Board (Highlight Reports).
vii. Measurement and monitoring of research progress and uncertainty [Ref 1, Ref 4].

#### V. Managing Product Delivery (MP)
This process focuses on the creation of the specialized research products (outputs), performed by the Team Manager or research team.
i. Accept work package from the Project Manager.
ii. Execute the work package (conduct research activities, experiments, data analysis).
iii. Deliver research products (reports, prototypes, documented findings).
iv. Report progress to the Project Manager.

#### VI. Managing a Stage Boundary (SB)
At the end of each stage, this process ensures the Project Board reviews progress and plans for the next stage.
i. Plan the next stage of research, adapting based on initial findings.
ii. Update the Project Plan and Business Case with actual progress and revised forecasts.
iii. Report stage end to the Project Board, requesting authorization for the next stage.

#### VII. Closing a Project (CP)
This phase provides a fixed point at which acceptance for the project's products is confirmed and project objectives achieved.
i. Hand over research products to the relevant operational team or stakeholders.
ii. Evaluate the project against its original objectives and expected benefits.
iii. Recommend project closure to the Project Board.
iv. Conduct post-project review, capturing lessons learned for future research [Ref 7].
v. Formally archive project documentation.

### Roles

For a small IT research project using PRINCE2, the following key roles are recommended:

*   **Executive:** The ultimate accountability for the project's success. Represents the business perspective.
*   **Senior User:** Represents the interests of those who will use the research product or benefit from its findings.
*   **Senior Supplier:** Represents the interests of those who provide specialist resources and expertise to the project (e.g., IT specialists, researchers).
*   **Project Manager:** Responsible for day-to-day management, planning, monitoring, and controlling the project stages within the agreed tolerances [Ref 10].
*   **Project Assurance:** Provides independent oversight to the Project Board (often a part-time role for a small project, potentially an external reviewer or an internal QA lead).
*   **Team Manager (Optional/Combined):** Manages the research team directly. For a small project, this role is often combined with the Project Manager.
*   **Project Support (Optional/Combined):** Provides administrative and technical support. For a small project, this is usually covered by the Project Manager or a dedicated researcher.
*   **Research Team Members:** Individuals performing the research tasks, experiments, and analysis.

### Decision Gates

Decision gates are critical points where the Project Board (Executive, Senior User, Senior Supplier) evaluates the project's viability and authorizes continuation [Ref 12]. These gates ensure proper governance and control, especially important for a small project where quick decisions are vital.

i.  **Project Mandate / Start-up Authorization:** Occurs after the initial Project Brief and Outline Business Case are presented. The Project Board decides whether to proceed with the Initiation Stage [Ref 12].
ii. **Project Initiation Authorization (PID Approval):** After the Initiation Stage, the Project Board reviews the comprehensive Project Initiation Documentation (PID). This is the point where the project is formally authorized to proceed into its delivery stages, based on a detailed plan, resources, and defined scope [Ref 1].
iii. **Stage Boundary Approvals:** At the end of each management stage, the Project Board reviews the current stage's performance, approves the next stage plan, and continues the project. This is crucial for a research project, allowing for adaptation based on interim findings [Ref 7, Ref 10].
iv. **Exception Authorization:** If a stage or the project is forecast to exceed its agreed tolerances (e.g., budget, schedule, scope deviation), the Project Manager must raise an Exception Report. The Project Board decides on corrective action, whether to amend plans, or even stop the project [Ref 11].
v. **Project Closure Authorization:** Upon completion of all deliverables and the final review, the Project Board authorizes the formal closure of the project, confirming that objectives have been met and products handed over [Ref 7].
