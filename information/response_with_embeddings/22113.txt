### Phases and Activities

The project will follow a predictive lifecycle model, segmented into five distinct phases, allowing for structured progression and control.

#### I. Project Initiation
The goal of this phase is to define the project at a high level and obtain formal authorization to proceed.
i.  Define Project Objectives: Clearly articulate the project's purpose and align it with organizational strategy and vision, considering potential outputs and outcomes [Ref 15, Ref 8].
ii. Identify and Analyze Stakeholders: Systematically identify all persons, groups, or organizations that can affect, be affected by, or perceive themselves to be affected by the project [Ref 3, Ref 8]. Develop an initial plan for effectively engaging with these stakeholders [Ref 12].
iii. Develop Project Charter: Create a document formally authorizing the project, outlining high-level project objectives, scope, and assigning the Project Manager the authority to apply organizational resources.
iv. Establish Initial Project Governance: Define the principles, policies, and framework by which the project will be directed and controlled [Ref 7, Ref 10], ensuring appropriate support and guidance [Ref 10].
v. Secure Project Sponsor: Confirm the person responsible for obtaining resources and executive decisions to enable project success [Ref 8].

#### II. Project Planning
This phase details how the project will be executed, monitored, and controlled, building quality into processes and deliverables [Ref 12].
i.  Define Product and Project Scope: Precisely define what the project will deliver (product scope) and the work required to deliver it (project scope) [Ref 1].
ii. Create Work Breakdown Structure (WBS): Decompose the defined scope into progressively lower levels, creating a comprehensive list of work packages [Ref 3].
iii. Estimate, Schedule, and Budget: Develop detailed estimates for activities, create a realistic project schedule, and establish the project budget [Ref 1].
iv. Plan Resources: Identify the required team composition, roles, and responsibilities, fostering a collaborative team environment [Ref 1, Ref 12]. Plan for any necessary procurement activities [Ref 1].
v. Plan for Risk Management: Identify, analyze, and plan responses to both threats (negative impacts) and opportunities (positive impacts) [Ref 3, Ref 14, Ref 12].
vi. Establish Change Management: Define the process for managing changes throughout the project lifecycle to control scope, schedule, and cost [Ref 1].
vii. Develop Communication Plan: Outline how project information will be exchanged, who will communicate what, when, and to whom [Ref 1].
viii. Finalize Project Management Plan: Integrate all subsidiary plans into a comprehensive document guiding project execution.

#### III. Project Execution
This phase involves carrying out the planned work to produce the project's deliverables [Ref 7].
i.  Direct and Manage Project Work: Execute the tasks defined in the Project Management Plan [Ref 2, Ref 8].
ii. Acquire and Develop Project Team: Onboard team members and facilitate their development, fostering a respectful and caring environment [Ref 1, Ref 12].
iii. Manage Stakeholder Engagement and Communication: Implement the communication plan and continuously engage with stakeholders to address their expectations and concerns [Ref 2, Ref 12].
iv. Conduct Procurement: Manage supplier relationships and ensure timely delivery of procured goods and services.
v. Implement Risk Responses: Execute the planned actions to mitigate threats and capitalize on opportunities [Ref 12].

#### IV. Monitoring & Control
This phase runs concurrently with Project Execution and ensures the project stays on track and within its defined parameters.
i.  Monitor Project Work and Performance: Continuously track progress against the project baseline and measure performance across various domains [Ref 2].
ii. Control Scope, Schedule, and Cost: Manage any deviations from the plan, ensuring the project remains within the approved scope, schedule, and budget [Ref 1].
iii. Perform Integrated Change Control: Review all change requests, approve or reject them, and manage approved changes to deliverables, organizational process assets, project documents, and the project management plan [Ref 1].
iv. Monitor and Control Risks: Continuously identify new risks, re-evaluate existing ones, and monitor the effectiveness of risk responses [Ref 3, Ref 14].
v. Control Quality: Conduct quality assurance and control activities to ensure deliverables meet specified quality requirements [Ref 12].
vi. Manage Issues: Address events that arise during the project that could impact its objectives [Ref 7].

#### V. Project Closure
The final phase formally concludes all project activities and releases resources.
i.  Verify and Obtain Final Deliverable Acceptance: Confirm that all unique and verifiable elements required to be produced by the project have been completed and formally accepted by the client/stakeholders [Ref 7].
ii. Conduct Final Performance Measurement and Reporting: Document the project's final performance against its objectives [Ref 2].
iii. Close Contracts and Procurements: Formally close all agreements with external vendors and suppliers.
iv. Release Project Resources: Formally release team members and other project resources [Ref 1].
v. Capture Lessons Learned: Document project successes, failures, and recommendations for future projects.
vi. Formal Project Closure: Obtain administrative and financial closure, signaling the project's formal end [Ref 6].

### Roles

The following key roles are essential for the successful delivery of this project:

i.  **Project Sponsor:** The person responsible for obtaining the resources and executive decisions to enable project success [Ref 8]. They provide strategic direction and champion the project within the organization.
ii. **Project Manager:** The individual coordinated activities to direct and control the accomplishment of agreed objectives [Ref 8]. They are responsible for the application of methods, tools, techniques, and competencies to enable the project to meet its objectives [Ref 4].
iii. **Project Team Members:** The individuals who perform the work of the project. A collaborative environment is crucial for success [Ref 12]. This includes IT specialists, construction technicians, and other relevant personnel.
iv. **Key Stakeholders:** Any person, group, or organization that has interests in, or can affect, be affected by, or perceive itself to be affected by, any aspect of a project [Ref 3, Ref 8]. This includes end-users, IT operations, facilities management, and leadership.
v. **Subject Matter Experts (SMEs):** Individuals providing specialized knowledge or expertise in specific areas, such as IT infrastructure, network security, or building codes.
vi. **Quality Assurance/Control Lead:** Responsible for ensuring that the project processes and deliverables adhere to established quality standards [Ref 12].

### Decision Gates

Decision gates are crucial checkpoints to ensure the project remains viable and aligned with objectives, providing formal approval points to proceed to the next phase. These points consider and plan for direction and justification [Ref 15].

i.  **Gate 1: Project Authorization (End of Initiation Phase)**
    *   **Purpose:** To confirm the project's strategic alignment and obtain formal approval to commit resources for detailed planning.
    *   **Decision Criteria:** Approved Project Charter, identified Project Sponsor and Project Manager, high-level feasibility.
    *   **Authority:** Project Sponsor [Ref 8] and key executive stakeholders.
    *   **Output:** Authorization to proceed to the Planning Phase.

ii. **Gate 2: Project Management Plan Approval (End of Planning Phase)**
    *   **Purpose:** To approve the comprehensive Project Management Plan, ensuring all aspects of the project are thoroughly planned and achievable within the constraints.
    *   **Decision Criteria:** Approved detailed scope, schedule, budget, risk management plan, communication plan, and resource plan [Ref 1].
    *   **Authority:** Project Sponsor [Ref 8] and potentially a Project Governance Board.
    *   **Output:** Authorization to execute the project work.

iii. **Gate 3: Milestone/Deliverable Acceptance (During Execution Phase)**
    *   **Purpose:** To formally accept key project deliverables or milestones, verifying their quality and completeness before proceeding with subsequent work [Ref 7].
    *   **Decision Criteria:** Verification that deliverables meet defined quality standards and stakeholder requirements [Ref 7, Ref 12].
    *   **Authority:** Project Manager, relevant Key Stakeholders (e.g., end-users, operations lead).
    *   **Output:** Formal sign-off on completed work packages or milestones.

iv. **Gate 4: Project Closure Authorization (End of Closure Phase)**
    *   **Purpose:** To formally conclude the project, confirming all objectives are met and administrative closure activities are completed [Ref 6].
    *   **Decision Criteria:** Formal acceptance of all project deliverables, completion of all contracts, final project report, and lessons learned documentation [Ref 2, Ref 7].
    *   **Authority:** Project Sponsor [Ref 8] and Project Manager.
    *   **Output:** Formal administrative closure of the project.
