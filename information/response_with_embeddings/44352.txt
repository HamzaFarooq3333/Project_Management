### Phases and Activities

The project will proceed through distinct phases, directly mirroring PRINCE2's process model, ensuring controlled progress and decision-making at critical junctures [Ref 10].

#### Phase I: Pre-Project – Starting Up a Project (SU)

This phase establishes the project's foundation, ensuring it is worthwhile and viable before significant resources are committed.

i.  **Appoint Executive and Project Manager:** Establish key leadership roles to define responsibilities and accountabilities early [Ref 5, Ref 6].
ii. **Capture Previous Lessons:** Review past project experiences to identify opportunities for improvement and avoid common pitfalls [Ref 10].
iii. **Design and Appoint Project Management Team:** Define the project team structure and assign roles (e.g., Team Managers, Project Support) based on project needs.
iv. **Prepare Outline Business Case:** Develop an initial justification for the project, outlining expected benefits and alignment with organizational strategy.
v.  **Select Project Approach and Assemble Project Brief:** Determine the overall strategy for delivering the project (e.g., specific marketing channels, technical platforms) and consolidate initial project information.
vi. **Plan the Initiation Stage:** Develop a detailed plan for the subsequent Initiation Stage, including activities, resources, and timelines.

#### Phase II: Initiation – Initiating a Project (IP)

This phase ensures a solid understanding of the project's scope, risks, and overall plan, moving from concept to a detailed, authorized blueprint.

i.  **Define Project Scope (Product and Project):** Clarify what the marketing campaign will deliver (product scope) and the work required to deliver it (project scope) [Ref 3].
ii. **Refine Business Case:** Develop a detailed Business Case, including costs, benefits, and timescales, to justify the project's continued investment.
iii. **Establish Project Controls:** Define how the project will be monitored and controlled, including reporting mechanisms, tolerances, and management stages.
iv. **Create Project Plan:** Develop a comprehensive plan including estimating, scheduling, budgeting, and resource allocation [Ref 3]. This involves defining the development approach and life cycle [Ref 2].
v.  **Plan for Quality, Risk, Communication, and Change Management:**
    *   **Quality Plan:** Define quality standards and how they will be achieved for marketing deliverables.
    *   **Risk Management Strategy:** Identify potential threats and opportunities [Ref 7, Ref 14], and plan responses. This includes considering the organization's risk attitude and appetite [Ref 14].
    *   **Communication Management Strategy:** Detail how information will be shared with stakeholders [Ref 3].
    *   **Change Management Strategy:** Establish the process for managing and controlling changes to the project baseline [Ref 3].
vi. **Develop Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) and Work Packages:** Decompose the defined scope into manageable components and work packages [Ref 4].

#### Phase III: Execution and Control – Directing a Project (DP), Controlling a Stage (CS), Managing Product Delivery (MP), Managing a Stage Boundary (SB)

This ongoing phase manages the daily work, tracks progress, and ensures the project remains on track through planned stages, typical for a project of this duration.

i.  **Authorize Stage or Exception Plan (DP/SB):** The Project Board reviews and authorizes each stage's plan or addresses exceptions.
ii. **Manage Stage Execution and Deliver Products (CS/MP):** The Project Manager oversees the day-to-day activities, allocating work to Team Managers, and ensuring product delivery according to specifications.
iii. **Monitor Project Work, Delivery, and Measurement (CS/DP):** Continuously track progress against the plan, measure performance, and report findings [Ref 2].
iv. **Manage Risks and Issues:** Implement risk responses, monitor new risks (threats and opportunities), and manage issues as they arise [Ref 14].
v.  **Manage Change:** Assess and control changes to the project baseline, ensuring only approved changes are implemented [Ref 3].
vi. **Report Progress to Stakeholders:** Regularly communicate project status, risks, and issues to relevant stakeholders [Ref 2, Ref 9].
vii. **Team Collaboration and Environment:** Foster a collaborative team environment and effectively engage with stakeholders [Ref 9].

#### Phase IV: Closure – Closing a Project (CP)

This final phase formally wraps up the project, ensuring all products are handed over, lessons are learned, and the project is officially terminated.

i.  **Decommission Project:** Ensure all project resources are released, and the project environment is closed down.
ii. **Identify Follow-on Actions:** Document any remaining work, recommendations for future actions, or unresolved issues to be handled post-project.
iii. **Handover Products:** Formally transfer ownership of the project's deliverables (e.g., marketing campaigns, digital assets) to the operational teams.
iv. **Evaluate Project Performance and Report Lessons Learned:** Assess how well the project performed against its objectives and document insights for future projects [Ref 11].
v.  **Formal Project Closure:** Obtain formal approval from the Project Board that the project is complete.

### Roles

PRINCE2 defines clear, structured roles essential for governance and efficient project delivery:

*   **Executive:** Accountable for the project's Business Case and overall success. Represents the business interests. [Ref 6 - *Sponsor is responsible for obtaining resources and executive decisions.*]
*   **Senior User:** Represents the interests of those who will use the project's products or benefit from the outcome.
*   **Senior Supplier:** Represents the interests of those designing, developing, facilitating, and implementing the project's products.
*   **Project Manager:** Responsible for the day-to-day management of the project within the boundaries set by the Project Board [Ref 15].
*   **Team Manager:** Responsible for managing a team to deliver specific work packages.
*   **Project Assurance:** An independent role providing oversight to the Project Board, ensuring the project is conducted correctly.
*   **Change Authority:** (Optional) Authorizes changes and approves exceptions.
*   **Project Support:** Provides administrative and logistical support to the Project Manager and team.
*   **Stakeholders:** Individuals, groups, or organizations affected by or perceiving themselves to be affected by the project [Ref 2, Ref 6]. Their effective engagement is critical [Ref 9].

### Decision Gates

Decision gates are crucial control points, particularly for a large, long-duration project, ensuring continued viability and strategic alignment [Ref 15]. These align with PRINCE2's principle of managing by stages.

i.  **Project Authorization (End of Pre-Project / Start-up):**
    *   **Purpose:** The Project Board reviews the Project Brief and the Initiation Stage Plan to decide whether to authorize the project's initiation.
    *   **Evidence:** This gate ensures that the project has a sound basis and a plan for detailed planning, preventing premature commitment of significant resources [Ref 10, Ref 15].

ii. **Stage Authorization (End of each Management Stage):**
    *   **Purpose:** At the end of each management stage, the Project Board reviews the End Stage Report, updated Business Case, and the next Stage Plan. They decide whether to authorize the next stage or to stop the project.
    *   **Evidence:** This iterative review allows for flexibility and adaptability throughout the project lifecycle, crucial for a project over 18 months, enabling "optimizing risk responses" and "navigating complexity" [Ref 9, Ref 14]. This also aligns with PMBOK's "Delivery" and "Measurement" domains [Ref 2].

iii. **Project Closure Authorization (End of Project):**
    *   **Purpose:** The Project Board reviews the End Project Report and Lessons Learned Report to formally confirm that the project has achieved its objectives (or that it is no longer viable) and authorizes its closure.
    *   **Evidence:** This final gate ensures the project is formally decommissioned, products are handed over, and valuable lessons are captured for future endeavors [Ref 15].
