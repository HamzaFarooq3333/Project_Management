### Phases and Activities

The project lifecycle will be managed through distinct phases, each with specific objectives and activities, aligning with PRINCE2 processes and PMBOK/ISO standards.

#### I. Pre-Project / Starting Up a Project (SU)
This phase focuses on the initial project concept, assessing viability, and establishing the foundational elements before committing significant resources.
i.  **Project Mandate and Initial Business Case Definition:** Develop the initial project mandate, outlining the high-level objectives and strategic justification for the project, aligning with organizational strategy [Ref 14].
ii. **Appointment of Key Roles:** Appoint the Executive (Project Sponsor) and Project Manager, establishing clear accountability [Ref 7], [Ref 11].
iii. **Stakeholder Identification and Analysis:** Identify and analyze all relevant stakeholders, including internal departments, regulatory bodies, and community groups, to ensure effective engagement [Ref 2], [Ref 6].
iv. **Develop Project Brief:** Create a high-level Project Brief, outlining the project's purpose, objectives, scope, and initial benefits, as well as high-level risks and issues.
v.  **Lessons Learned Review:** Review lessons from previous similar projects to inform initial planning and avoid known pitfalls.

#### II. Initiation / Initiating a Project (IP)
This phase involves detailed planning and establishing the project management framework for the entire project.
i.  **Develop Project Initiation Documentation (PID):** Create the PID, which serves as the foundational document for the project. This includes a detailed Business Case, Project Plan, Quality Management Approach, Risk Management Approach, Configuration Management Approach, Communication Management Approach, and Tailoring documentation [Ref 1].
ii. **Detailed Planning (Scope, Schedule, Budget):** Conduct comprehensive planning, including defining the product and project scope, estimating costs, developing a detailed schedule, and establishing the budget [Ref 1].
iii. **Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) Creation:** Decompose the defined scope into progressively lower levels of work packages, ensuring a clear understanding of all required deliverables and activities [Ref 4].
iv. **Risk Management Strategy Development:** Establish a proactive approach to identify, assess, and plan responses for individual and overall project risks (opportunities and threats), considering the organization's risk appetite [Ref 10].
v.  **Quality Management Strategy Development:** Define how quality will be assured and controlled throughout the project, ensuring deliverables meet specified requirements, critical for a healthcare facility [Ref 6].
vi. **Procurement Strategy:** Plan for all necessary external acquisitions, outlining the procurement approach and contracts required [Ref 1].

#### III. Stage Management & Execution / Directing, Controlling, Managing Product Delivery, Managing Stage Boundary (DP, CS, MPD, SB)
This is the iterative core of the project, focusing on delivering the products in controlled stages.
i.  **Directing the Project (DP):** The Project Board provides overall direction and control, authorizing stages and making key decisions [Ref 9].
ii. **Controlling a Stage (CS):** The Project Manager manages each stage on a day-to-day basis, authorizing work packages [Ref 4], monitoring progress against plans, and managing issues and risks [Ref 10].
iii. **Managing Product Delivery (MPD):** Team Managers oversee the creation and delivery of specialist products according to specifications and tolerances. Deliverables are unique and verifiable elements produced by the project [Ref 8].
iv. **Monitoring and Reporting:** Continuously monitor project performance, including progress, budget, schedule, and quality. Regularly report status to the Project Board and relevant stakeholders [Ref 2].
v.  **Issue and Change Management:** Identify, assess, and manage issues and changes to the project, evaluating their impact on the Business Case, plans, and risks [Ref 1].
vi. **Managing Stage Boundaries (SB):** At the end of each stage, review performance, update the Business Case, and prepare the plan for the next stage. This ensures continued project viability and alignment with objectives [Ref 7], [Ref 9].
vii. **Tailoring and Adaptability:** Apply project management principles and practices, tailoring them to the specific context of each stage and evolving project needs [Ref 6], [Ref 7].

#### IV. Project Closure / Closing a Project (CP)
This phase ensures the formal completion of the project and the handover of its products.
i.  **Product Handover and Acceptance:** Formally hand over the completed healthcare facility and associated deliverables to the operational team, securing formal acceptance [Ref 7], [Ref 8].
ii. **Post-Project Review and Lessons Learned:** Conduct a comprehensive review of the project's performance, documenting successes, failures, and lessons learned for future projects [Ref 7], [Ref 2].
iii. **Project Decommissioning:** Release project resources, disband the project team, and formally close contracts.
iv. **Final Project Report:** Prepare a final report summarizing project performance, outcomes, and recommendations.

### Roles

Key roles are essential for clear accountability and effective project governance in a PRINCE2 environment.

*   **Executive (Project Sponsor):** Ultimately accountable for the project's success and alignment with the organizational strategy. Responsible for the Business Case and securing resources [Ref 11], [Ref 14]. Chairs the Project Board.
*   **Senior User Representative:** Represents the interests of the future users of the project's products (e.g., hospital management, clinical staff). Ensures the project delivers the required functionality and realizes benefits [Ref 6].
*   **Senior Supplier Representative:** Represents the interests of the external and internal teams responsible for producing the specialist products (e.g., main contractor, design consultants). Ensures the products are delivered to specifications.
*   **Project Manager:** Responsible for the day-to-day management of the project, planning, monitoring, and controlling all aspects, and delivering the project products within agreed tolerances [Ref 1], [Ref 11].
*   **Team Manager(s):** Manages a team of specialists to produce specific work packages or deliverables, reporting progress to the Project Manager [Ref 4]. In a construction project, this might include a Construction Manager, Design Manager, etc.
*   **Project Assurance:** Provides an independent oversight function for the Project Board, monitoring the project's performance and compliance with standards.
*   **Project Support:** Provides administrative support, advice, and guidance to the Project Manager and team.
*   **Stakeholders:** Individuals, groups, or organizations affected by or perceiving themselves to be affected by the project, including regulatory bodies, community representatives, and clinical staff [Ref 2], [Ref 6], [Ref 11].

### Decision Gates

Crucial decision gates are embedded throughout the project lifecycle to ensure continued viability, control, and alignment with project objectives and organizational strategy. These points typically involve the Project Board.

*   **Project Mandate Approval:** This is the initial go/no-go decision to proceed with the Pre-Project phase, authorizing the resources to develop the Project Brief. It ensures strategic alignment before significant investment [Ref 14].
*   **Project Initiation Approval (PID Sign-off):** At the end of the Initiation phase, the Project Board formally reviews and approves the Project Initiation Documentation (PID). This gate authorizes the project to proceed into its first delivery stage, confirming the project's viability, plan, and governance structure [Ref 9].
*   **End Stage Assessment (Per Stage):** At the end of each management stage, the Project Board reviews the completed stage, assesses performance, verifies the updated Business Case, and approves the plan for the next stage. This iterative gate is critical for controlling a large, multi-stage project, allowing for adjustments and maintaining control over time and resources [Ref 7], [Ref 9].
*   **Exception Report Approval:** If a stage or the project goes outside agreed tolerances (e.g., budget, schedule, scope), an Exception Report is raised. This gate requires the Project Board to decide on a course of action to bring the project back on track, or to formally agree to the deviation.
*   **Project Closure Approval:** The final decision gate, where the Project Board formally accepts the project's products, confirms that project objectives have been met, and authorizes the formal closure of the project [Ref 7].
