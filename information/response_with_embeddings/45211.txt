### Phases and Activities

The project will follow a predictive life cycle, characteristic of PMBOK, divided into sequential phases: Initiating, Planning, Executing, Monitoring & Controlling, and Closing.

#### I. Initiating Phase
This phase defines a new project or a new phase of an existing project by obtaining authorization to start.
i.  **Develop Project Charter**: Formally authorize the project and grant the Project Manager the authority to apply organizational resources to project activities.
ii. **Identify Stakeholders**: Identify all people or organizations impacted by the project, document their interests, involvement, and impact [Ref 2, Ref 8]. Establish initial engagement strategies [Ref 12].

#### II. Planning Phase
This phase progressively elaborates the project scope, objectives, and course of action required to attain the objectives. It is an iterative process [Ref 1].
i.  **Develop Project Management Plan**: Create a comprehensive document integrating all subsidiary plans.
ii. **Plan Scope Management**: Define how the project scope will be defined, validated, and controlled.
iii. **Define Scope**: Develop a detailed description of the project and product.
iv. **Create Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)**: Decompose the defined scope into progressively lower levels of work packages [Ref 5].
v.  **Plan Schedule Management**: Establish policies, procedures, and documentation for planning, developing, managing, executing, and controlling the project schedule.
vi. **Define Activities**: Identify specific actions to be performed to produce project deliverables.
vii. **Sequence Activities**: Identify and document relationships among project activities.
viii. **Estimate Activity Durations**: Estimate the number of work periods needed to complete individual activities [Ref 1].
ix. **Develop Schedule**: Analyze activity sequences, durations, resource requirements, and schedule constraints to create the project schedule [Ref 1].
x.  **Plan Cost Management**: Establish policies, procedures, and documentation for planning, managing, expending, and controlling project costs.
xi. **Estimate Costs**: Develop an approximation of the monetary resources needed to complete project activities [Ref 1].
xii. **Determine Budget**: Aggregate the estimated costs of individual activities or work packages to establish an authorized cost baseline [Ref 1].
xiii. **Plan Quality Management**: Identify quality requirements and/or standards for the project and product, and document how the project will demonstrate compliance. Emphasize building quality into processes and deliverables [Ref 12].
xiv. **Plan Resource Management**: Define how to estimate, acquire, manage, and utilize physical and team resources. Determine team composition [Ref 1].
xv. **Plan Communications Management**: Develop an appropriate approach and plan for project communications based on stakeholders' information needs and requirements [Ref 1].
xvi. **Plan Risk Management**: Define how to conduct risk management activities for the project, identifying potential threats [Ref 5, Ref 9] and opportunities, and planning responses to optimize risk [Ref 12].
xvii. **Perform Qualitative/Quantitative Risk Analysis**: Prioritize risks for further analysis or action by assessing their probability of occurrence and impact.
xviii. **Plan Risk Responses**: Develop options and actions to enhance opportunities and reduce threats to project objectives.
xix. **Plan Procurement Management**: Document project procurement decisions, specifying the approach and identifying potential sellers [Ref 1].
xx. **Plan Stakeholder Engagement**: Develop approaches to involve project stakeholders based on their needs, interests, and potential impact on project success [Ref 2, Ref 12].
xxi. **Plan Change Management**: Define the process for requesting, analyzing, and approving changes to project baselines [Ref 1].
xxii. **Establish Measurement Baselines**: Define how project performance will be measured against established baselines for scope, schedule, and cost [Ref 1, Ref 2].

#### III. Executing Phase
This phase performs the work defined in the project management plan to achieve project objectives.
i.  **Direct and Manage Project Work**: Lead and perform the work defined in the project management plan and implement approved changes.
ii. **Manage Project Knowledge**: Use existing knowledge and create new knowledge to achieve the project's objectives and contribute to organizational learning.
iii. **Manage Quality**: Translate the quality management plan into executable quality activities that incorporate the organization's quality policies into the project.
iv. **Acquire Resources**: Obtain team members, facilities, equipment, materials, supplies, and other resources.
v.  **Develop Team**: Improve competencies, team member interaction, and overall team environment to enhance project performance. Create a collaborative environment [Ref 12].
vi. **Manage Team**: Track team member performance, provide feedback, resolve issues, and manage team changes to optimize project performance.
vii. **Manage Communications**: Ensure timely and appropriate collection, creation, distribution, storage, retrieval, management, monitoring, and ultimate disposition of project information.
viii. **Implement Risk Responses**: Execute planned risk response strategies.
ix. **Conduct Procurements**: Obtain seller responses, select a seller, and award a contract [Ref 1].
x.  **Manage Stakeholder Engagement**: Communicate and work with stakeholders to meet their needs and expectations, address issues, and foster appropriate stakeholder involvement [Ref 12].

#### IV. Monitoring & Controlling Phase
This phase tracks, reviews, and regulates the progress and performance of the project; identifies any areas in which changes to the plan are required; and initiates the corresponding changes.
i.  **Monitor and Control Project Work**: Track, review, and report overall project progress to meet the performance objectives defined in the project management plan [Ref 1].
ii. **Perform Integrated Change Control**: Review all change requests, approve changes, and manage changes to deliverables, organizational process assets, project documents, and the project management plan [Ref 1].
iii. **Validate Scope**: Formalize acceptance of the completed project deliverables.
iv. **Control Scope**: Monitor the status of the project and product scope and manage changes to the scope baseline.
v.  **Control Schedule**: Monitor the status of project activities to update project progress and manage changes to the schedule baseline.
vi. **Control Costs**: Monitor the status of the project to update the project costs and manage changes to the cost baseline.
vii. **Control Quality**: Monitor and record results of executing the quality management activities to assess performance and recommend necessary changes.
viii. **Control Resources**: Ensure that the physical resources assigned and allocated to the project are available as planned, as well as monitor the planned versus actual utilization of resources.
ix. **Monitor Communications**: Ensure the information needs of the project and its stakeholders are met.
x.  **Monitor Risks**: Monitor the implementation of agreed-upon risk response plans, track identified risks, identify and analyze new risks, and evaluate risk process effectiveness [Ref 12].
xi. **Control Procurements**: Manage procurement relationships, monitor contract performance, and make changes and corrections as appropriate.
xii. **Monitor Stakeholder Engagement**: Monitor overall project stakeholder relationships and adjust strategies and plans for engaging stakeholders.
xiii. **Measurement**: Continuously measure project performance against planned baselines [Ref 1, Ref 2].

#### V. Closing Phase
This phase formally completes the project, phase, or contract.
i.  **Close Project or Phase**: Finalize all activities across all Project Management Process Groups to formally close the project or phase.
ii. **Formal Deliverable Acceptance**: Obtain formal acceptance of deliverables [Ref 7].
iii. **Lessons Learned**: Compile and archive project lessons learned for future organizational knowledge.
iv. **Archive Project Documents**: Finalize all project documentation and archive for historical records.
v.  **Release Resources**: Release project team members and other resources.

### Roles

The following key roles are essential for the successful execution of this IT infrastructure project:

i.  **Project Sponsor**: The person responsible for obtaining resources and executive decisions to enable project success [Ref 8, Ref 17]. Provides overall project direction and governance [Ref 7, Ref 10].
ii. **Project Manager**: The individual responsible for applying methods, tools, techniques, and competencies to enable the project to meet its objectives [Ref 4]. Leads the project team, manages day-to-day activities, and is accountable for project success.
iii. **Project Team Members**: Individuals involved in the project, responsible for executing work packages and activities as assigned [Ref 1]. This includes technical leads, infrastructure engineers, network specialists, system administrators, and other subject matter experts (SMEs).
iv. **Key Stakeholders**: Persons, groups, or organizations that have interests in, or can affect, be affected by, or perceive themselves to be affected by, any aspect of the project [Ref 2, Ref 5, Ref 8]. This includes end-users, IT operations, security teams, vendors, and potentially compliance officers.
v.  **Functional Managers**: Managers of organizational departments who provide resources (human and otherwise) to the project team.
vi. **Vendor/Contract Manager**: (If applicable) Responsible for overseeing third-party contracts and deliverables [Ref 1].

### Decision Gates

Decision gates are critical points in the project life cycle where formal reviews are conducted to assess project performance and progress, and to make informed decisions about continuing, redirecting, or terminating the project. These gates align with the project's life cycle model and project governance principles [Ref 6, Ref 7, Ref 10].

i.  **Project Charter Approval**:
    *   **Description**: Formal approval of the Project Charter by the Project Sponsor and relevant executive stakeholders. This authorizes the project to proceed and empowers the Project Manager.
    *   **Purpose**: Ensure alignment with organizational strategy and secure initial funding and resources.
    *   **Evidence**: Approved Project Charter, confirmed funding.
    *   **Reference**: Project authorization and sponsor's role [Ref 8].

ii. **Project Management Plan (PMP) Baseline Approval**:
    *   **Description**: Formal review and approval of the comprehensive Project Management Plan, including scope, schedule, and cost baselines, by the Project Sponsor and key stakeholders.
    *   **Purpose**: Confirm a complete and viable plan, securing commitment to the project's direction and performance measurement framework [Ref 1].
    *   **Evidence**: Signed-off PMP, including WBS, schedule, budget, risk plan, communication plan.
    *   **Reference**: Detailed planning elements [Ref 1], importance of baselines for measurement [Ref 1, Ref 2].

iii. **Phase Gate Reviews (e.g., Design Complete, Build Complete, Test Complete)**:
    *   **Description**: At the end of major project phases (e.g., requirements, design, implementation, testing for an IT infrastructure project), a formal review is conducted.
    *   **Purpose**: To assess deliverables against acceptance criteria, review risks, evaluate performance to date, and decide whether to proceed to the next phase, re-plan, or terminate.
    *   **Evidence**: Approved phase deliverables, updated risk register, performance reports, updated business case.
    *   **Reference**: Project life cycle definition and phases [Ref 6, Ref 10], quality into deliverables [Ref 12].

iv. **Readiness for Deployment/Go-Live Approval**:
    *   **Description**: A comprehensive review prior to the operational deployment of the IT infrastructure. This involves all critical stakeholders including operations, security, and business users.
    *   **Purpose**: To ensure the solution meets functional and non-functional requirements, operational readiness, security compliance, and stakeholder acceptance, and to mitigate remaining risks.
    *   **Evidence**: User Acceptance Test (UAT) sign-off, security audit reports, operational readiness plan, contingency plan, stakeholder acceptance documentation.
    *   **Reference**: Project delivery and stakeholder engagement [Ref 2, Ref 12].

v.  **Project Closure Approval**:
    *   **Description**: Formal sign-off by the Project Sponsor confirming that all project work has been completed, deliverables have been accepted, and objectives have been met.
    *   **Purpose**: Formally close the project, release resources, and initiate post-project activities like benefits realization management.
    *   **Evidence**: Formal acceptance of final deliverables [Ref 7], lessons learned documentation, final performance report.
    *   **Reference**: Project life cycle conclusion [Ref 6, Ref 10].
