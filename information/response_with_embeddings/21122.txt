### Phases and Activities

#### I. Starting Up a Project (Pre-Project)
This phase establishes the foundational elements required before committing to significant planning, ensuring a viable project worth initiating.

i.  **Appoint Project Management Team:** Define the Project Board (Executive, Senior User, Senior Supplier) and Project Manager [Ref 5].
ii. **Capture Project Mandate:** Understand the initial business idea and terms of reference.
iii. **Design and Appoint Project Management Team:** Determine the structure of the project team, including the Development Team, considering team composition [Ref 2] and the need for a collaborative environment [Ref 7].
iv. **Prepare the Outline Business Case:** Develop an initial justification for the project, outlining expected benefits and costs.
v.  **Select Project Approach and Assemble Project Brief:** Decide on the high-level development approach (e.g., iterative software development within PRINCE2 stages) [Ref 3], and compile initial project information.

#### II. Initiating a Project (Planning & Setup)
This phase focuses on detailed planning and establishing the project's baseline, including scope, schedule, budget, and risks.

i.  **Define Project and Product Scope:** Clearly articulate what the software product will deliver and what the project encompasses [Ref 2]. This includes defining deliverables [Ref 9].
ii. **Develop a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS):** Decompose the defined scope into progressively lower levels of work elements, including work packages for the development team [Ref 4].
iii. **Estimate and Schedule Project Activities:** Based on the WBS, estimate effort, duration, and cost, leading to a project schedule and budget [Ref 2]. For software development, this may involve high-level estimates for early stages and more detailed estimates for subsequent iterative stages.
iv. **Plan Quality, Risk, and Communication:** Establish how quality will be built into processes and deliverables [Ref 7], identify and plan responses to threats and risks [Ref 2], [Ref 4], [Ref 14], and define communication channels and plans for engaging effectively with stakeholders [Ref 2], [Ref 7], [Ref 8].
v.  **Prepare the Project Initiation Documentation (PID):** Consolidate all planning information into a comprehensive document that defines the project's baseline. This includes the detailed Business Case, Project Plan, Quality Management Approach, Risk Management Approach, Communication Management Approach, and Change Control Approach [Ref 2].
vi. **Set up Project Controls:** Define how the project will be monitored and controlled (e.g., reporting, issue management [Ref 9], change management [Ref 2]).

#### III. Controlling a Stage & Managing Product Delivery (Iterative Development Stages)
This represents the core execution and delivery phase, broken into manageable stages for iterative software development. For a small project, 2-3 stages might be appropriate.

i.  **Authorize Work Package:** The Project Manager authorizes the Development Team to execute specific work packages derived from the WBS [Ref 4].
ii. **Execute Work Package:** The Development Team carries out the actual software development activities (e.g., design, coding, testing) as per the authorized work package. This involves building quality into processes and deliverables [Ref 7].
iii. **Monitor Progress and Report:** The Project Manager regularly monitors progress against the stage plan, reviews actuals versus planned, manages issues [Ref 9] and risks [Ref 4], and prepares regular reports for the Project Board. Measurement is a key performance domain here [Ref 1].
iv. **Manage Stage Boundaries (Internal):** Periodically review the stage's progress against performance targets and the Business Case. This includes reviewing the stage plan, budget, and risks, and making necessary adjustments.
v.  **Conduct Quality Checks and Reviews:** Perform internal reviews, testing, and quality assurance activities to ensure deliverables meet defined quality criteria [Ref 7].
vi. **Manage Change:** Implement a process for handling change requests, assessing their impact on scope, schedule, and budget [Ref 2], and obtaining approval before implementation.

#### IV. Managing a Stage Boundary (End-Stage Assessment)
At the end of each delivery stage, a formal review is conducted to assess performance, update the Business Case, and plan for the next stage.

i.  **Assess Current Stage Performance:** Review the current stage's performance against its plan, updating the project records.
ii. **Update Project Business Case:** Reconfirm the project's continued viability and justification against organizational strategy [Ref 15], updating the Business Case with actuals and refined forecasts [Ref 13].
iii. **Plan Next Stage:** If the project is still justified, plan the next stage in detail, including work packages, resources, and revised estimates.
iv. **Report End-Stage Status to Project Board:** Present the assessment, updated Business Case, and next stage plan to the Project Board for approval to proceed.

#### V. Closing a Project
This phase ensures the formal closure of the project, including handing over the product, reviewing performance, and archiving project information.

i.  **Prepare Planned Closure:** Plan activities for project closure, including formal handover of the software product.
ii. **Hand Over Product:** Formally transfer the completed software product to the operational team or client.
iii. **Evaluate Project Performance:** Review the project's performance against its objectives, documenting lessons learned [Ref 10], and identifying areas for improvement. This contributes to future organizational learning.
iv. **Recommend Project Closure:** Present the final project status and lessons learned to the Project Board, recommending formal project closure.
v.  **Archive Project Documentation:** Securely store all project records and documentation.

### Roles

*   **Executive (Project Board):** Overall business ownership and accountability, ensuring the project aligns with organizational objectives and delivers value [Ref 7], [Ref 15]. Ultimately accountable for the project's success.
*   **Senior User (Project Board):** Represents the interests of the users of the project's product, ensuring the solution meets their needs and is fit for purpose.
*   **Senior Supplier (Project Board):** Represents the interests of those designing, developing, facilitating, and implementing the project's product, ensuring the technical feasibility and quality of deliverables.
*   **Project Manager:** Responsible for day-to-day management of the project, ensuring it delivers products within the specified tolerances of time, cost, quality, scope, benefits, and risk. Directly accountable for planning, monitoring, and controlling project work [Ref 1], [Ref 2].
*   **Development Team (Team Manager/Developer):** Responsible for producing the project's specialist products (software) according to quality specifications, within agreed work packages [Ref 4].
*   **Project Assurance:** Independent oversight role, often reporting to the Project Board, ensuring the project is being conducted correctly.
*   **Project Support:** Provides administrative and coordination services to the project manager and team.

### Decision Gates

i.  **Project Authorization (End of I. Starting Up a Project):** The Project Board reviews the Project Brief and Outline Business Case to decide whether to authorize the initiation of the project. This gate ensures initial justification and strategic alignment [Ref 15].
ii. **Project Initiation Authorization (End of II. Initiating a Project):** The Project Board reviews the Project Initiation Documentation (PID) to confirm the project's viability and approve the Project Plan for the first stage. This is a critical point for project governance [Ref 13].
iii. **End-Stage Assessments (End of IV. Managing a Stage Boundary):** At the end of each delivery stage, the Project Board reviews the Stage Report, updated Business Case, and the next Stage Plan. This is a formal decision gate to determine if the project should continue, be modified, or be stopped, based on its continued justification and performance [Ref 13], [Ref 15].
iv. **Project Closure Authorization (End of V. Closing a Project):** The Project Board reviews the End Project Report and Lessons Learned Report, formally confirming that the project objectives have been met and approving the final closure of the project.
