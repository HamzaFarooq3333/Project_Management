### Phases and Activities

The project lifecycle will be divided into five distinct phases, ensuring structured progress and control points.

#### I. Initiation Phase
This phase defines the project at a high level and obtains authorization.
i.  **Project Mandate/Charter Development:** Define the project's purpose, high-level objectives, initial scope, and assign a Project Manager. This establishes the initial project governance [Ref 11].
ii. **Stakeholder Identification:** Identify all internal and external parties who can affect, be affected by, or perceive themselves to be affected by the project [Ref 5, Ref 13]. This includes healthcare staff, patients, regulatory bodies, and internal IT/facilities.
iii. **Initial Risk Assessment:** Conduct a preliminary identification of potential risks (threats and opportunities) [Ref 10].
iv. **Feasibility Review:** Confirm the project's viability and alignment with organizational objectives.

#### II. Planning Phase
This phase refines objectives and plans the course of action required to attain them.
i.  **Scope Definition & Work Breakdown Structure (WBS):** Clearly define product and project scope, breaking down deliverables into progressively lower levels (work packages) [Ref 1, Ref 5]. This is crucial for managing expectations and preventing ambiguity [Ref 6].
ii. **Activity Definition & Sequencing:** Detail the specific activities required to produce deliverables and establish their logical order.
iii. **Resource Planning:** Determine the necessary team composition, equipment, materials, and infrastructure resources [Ref 1, Ref 14].
iv. **Estimating & Scheduling:** Estimate duration, effort, and cost for activities and develop a project schedule and budget [Ref 1].
v.  **Risk Management Planning:** Develop strategies for optimizing responses to identified individual and overall project risks, considering the organization's risk appetite [Ref 10].
vi. **Quality Planning:** Define quality standards and metrics, building quality into processes and deliverables from the outset [Ref 4]. This is critical for healthcare infrastructure.
vii. **Communication Planning:** Develop a communication plan outlining what, when, to whom, and how information will be shared [Ref 1]. Essential for effective stakeholder engagement [Ref 4].
viii. **Procurement Planning:** Identify external resources or services required and plan their acquisition [Ref 1].
ix. **Change Management Planning:** Establish processes for managing project changes, ensuring they are evaluated, approved, and communicated [Ref 1].
x.  **Tailoring & Baselining:** Adjust management processes to fit the project's context (small size, healthcare industry) and establish the project management plan baseline [Ref 4, Ref 7].

#### III. Execution Phase
This phase carries out the planned work to achieve project objectives.
i.  **Direct & Manage Project Work:** Execute planned activities, producing project deliverables [Ref 2, Ref 8].
ii. **Team Management & Development:** Build a collaborative project team environment and manage team performance [Ref 4].
iii. **Stakeholder Engagement:** Continuously engage with stakeholders, managing their expectations and ensuring their involvement [Ref 4].
iv. **Resource Acquisition & Management:** Obtain and manage project resources as planned [Ref 14].
v.  **Procurement Management:** Oversee vendor performance and contract compliance.

#### IV. Monitoring & Control Phase
This phase tracks, reviews, and regulates the progress and performance of the project; identifies any areas in which changes to the plan are required; and initiates those changes. This phase runs concurrently with Execution.
i.  **Monitor & Control Project Work:** Measure project performance against the baseline [Ref 1, Ref 2].
ii. **Perform Integrated Change Control:** Review all change requests, approve changes, and manage changes to deliverables, organizational process assets, and the project management plan [Ref 1].
iii. **Risk Monitoring & Control:** Continuously monitor for new risks, re-evaluate existing risks, and implement risk responses [Ref 10].
iv. **Quality Control:** Monitor specific project results to determine whether they comply with relevant quality standards and identify ways to eliminate causes of unsatisfactory performance [Ref 4].
v.  **Issue Management:** Identify and resolve issues that arise during the project [Ref 8].
vi. **Performance Reporting:** Regularly report on project status, progress, and forecasts to stakeholders.

#### V. Closure Phase
This phase formally completes all project activities and releases resources.
i.  **Final Deliverable Acceptance:** Obtain formal acceptance of the final project deliverables from the customer/sponsor.
ii. **Administrative Closure:** Finalize all activities across all process groups to formally close the project or phase.
iii. **Contract Closure:** Complete and settle each contract, including the resolution of any open items.
iv. **Lessons Learned & Knowledge Transfer:** Document lessons learned and update organizational process assets for future projects.
v.  **Resource Release:** Formally release project resources.

### Roles

The following key roles are essential for the successful delivery of this project:

i.  **Project Sponsor:** The person responsible for obtaining the resources and executive decisions to enable success [Ref 13]. Provides overall governance and strategic direction [Ref 11].
ii. **Project Manager:** The individual responsible for coordinating activities to direct and control the accomplishment of agreed objectives [Ref 13, Ref 14]. Serves as a diligent, respectful, and caring steward [Ref 4].
iii. **Core Project Team:** Individuals directly involved in performing the project work and producing deliverables. They operate in a collaborative environment [Ref 4].
iv. **Healthcare Operations Lead:** Represents the end-users and ensures project alignment with clinical and operational needs.
v.  **Facilities/Technical Lead:** Provides expertise on infrastructure design, construction, and compliance.
vi. **Regulatory & Compliance Specialist:** Ensures adherence to all relevant healthcare regulations and ISO standards.
vii. **Key Stakeholders:** Person, group or organization that has interests in, or can affect, be affected by, or perceive itself to be affected by, any aspect of the project [Ref 5, Ref 13].

### Decision Gates

Crucial decision gates ensure governance and progression through the project lifecycle [Ref 11]. These gates involve formal review and approval by the Project Sponsor and relevant stakeholders.

i.  **Project Charter Approval:** At the end of the Initiation Phase. This formally authorizes the project to proceed to detailed planning. Decision to proceed with planning.
ii. **Project Management Plan (PMP) Baseline Approval:** At the end of the Planning Phase. This formally approves the scope, schedule, budget, and other subsidiary plans, establishing the performance baseline against which the project will be measured [Ref 1]. Decision to commence execution.
iii. **Key Deliverable Acceptance (Mid-project, if applicable):** After significant milestones or the completion of major deliverables during the Execution Phase. For infrastructure, this could be design approval or completion of a specific construction phase. Decision to proceed to the next major phase of execution.
iv. **Final Deliverable Acceptance:** At the end of the Execution Phase, prior to formal closure. This confirms that all project objectives have been met and the product or service is accepted by the customer/sponsor. Decision to move to project closure.
v.  **Project Closure Approval:** At the end of the Closure Phase. This formally confirms the project is complete, all administrative and contractual obligations are met, and resources can be released. Decision to officially close the project.
