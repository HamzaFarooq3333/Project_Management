### Phases and Activities

The project lifecycle is divided into five distinct phases, each with specific objectives and activities, ensuring a disciplined approach to project delivery.

#### I. Project Initiation
This phase establishes the foundational elements of the project, defining its purpose and securing authorization.

i.  **Define Project Objectives and Scope:** Clearly articulate what the project aims to achieve, establishing the "one or more defined objectives" for this temporary endeavor [Ref 7]. This also includes initial high-level scope definition, considering the product and project scope [Ref 2].
ii. **Identify Stakeholders:** Conduct a thorough analysis to identify all persons, groups, or organizations that have interests in, or can affect, be affected by, or perceive themselves to be affected by, any aspect of the project [Ref 7, Ref 5].
iii. **Appoint Project Sponsor and Project Manager:** Formally assign the Project Sponsor, who is responsible for obtaining resources and executive decisions [Ref 7], and the Project Manager, who will lead the project.
iv. **Establish Project Governance:** Define the principles, policies, and procedures by which the project will be authorized and directed to accomplish agreed objectives [Ref 14]. This includes setting up project organization and reporting lines.
v. **Develop Project Charter:** Document the project's purpose, objectives, high-level scope, key stakeholders, and initial budget/timeline, securing formal authorization to proceed.

#### II. Project Planning
This phase involves developing a comprehensive plan that guides the project's execution and control, detailing "what, how, when, and by whom" the project objectives will be met [Ref 2].

i.  **Detailed Scope Definition and WBS Creation:** Elaborate on the product and project scope [Ref 2]. Develop a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS), which is a decomposition of the defined scope into progressively lower levels of work elements [Ref 3].
ii. **Estimating, Scheduling, and Budgeting:** Perform detailed estimating and scheduling of activities and resource requirements. Develop a comprehensive budget [Ref 2].
iii. **Team Composition and Resource Planning:** Determine the team composition and plan for resource acquisition and allocation [Ref 2, Ref 12].
iv. **Communication Plan Development:** Create a strategy for effective information flow among stakeholders [Ref 2].
v. **Risk Management Planning:** Identify potential threats (risk occurrences that would have a negative impact) [Ref 3, Ref 9], assess their likelihood and impact, and plan appropriate responses to optimize risk responses [Ref 15].
vi. **Quality Management Planning:** Define processes and standards to build quality into processes and deliverables [Ref 15].
vii. **Procurement Planning:** Identify external resources needed and plan for their acquisition [Ref 2].
viii. **Change Management Planning:** Establish a process for managing and controlling changes to the project baseline [Ref 2, Ref 15].
ix. **Tailoring Project Processes:** Adjust the chosen project management processes and methods to fit the specific context, complexity, and delivery approach (e.g., iterative for software development) [Ref 1, Ref 6, Ref 15].

#### III. Project Execution (Project Work & Delivery)
This phase focuses on carrying out the planned work to produce the project's deliverables.

i.  **Direct and Manage Project Work:** Execute the project work according to the project plan [Ref 5, Ref 12]. This involves leading the project team, managing resources, and motivating individuals [Ref 12].
ii. **Implement Development Approach:** Apply the selected delivery approach (e.g., iterative, adaptive, or hybrid) for software development, as allowed by ISO21502 [Ref 1].
iii. **Perform Quality Assurance:** Ensure that planned quality standards are met throughout the development lifecycle, building quality into processes and deliverables [Ref 15].
iv. **Manage Stakeholder Engagement:** Effectively engage with stakeholders, addressing their expectations and fostering collaboration [Ref 15].
v. **Conduct Procurements:** Administer any necessary contracts with external vendors.
vi. **Team Development:** Support the project team environment and facilitate its growth [Ref 15].

#### IV. Project Monitoring and Controlling
This phase involves overseeing project progress, identifying deviations from the plan, and taking corrective actions.

i.  **Monitor Project Work:** Track the progress against the established baselines (scope, schedule, cost) [Ref 12].
ii. **Control Scope, Schedule, and Cost:** Manage changes to ensure the project remains on track and within approved parameters [Ref 2].
iii. **Measure Project Performance:** Regularly measure and report on project performance domains [Ref 5] to assess progress and forecast future outcomes [Ref 2].
iv. **Perform Quality Control:** Verify that deliverables conform to quality requirements.
v. **Monitor and Control Risks:** Continuously identify new risks, monitor existing ones, and implement planned risk responses to navigate complexity and optimize risk responses [Ref 3, Ref 9, Ref 15].
vi. **Manage Communications:** Ensure information is distributed effectively according to the communication plan.
vii. **Control Procurements:** Administer procurement contracts to ensure performance.
viii. **Integrated Change Control:** Review and approve/reject all change requests, assessing their impact across the project.

#### V. Project Closure
This final phase involves formally completing the project, obtaining final acceptance, and reviewing lessons learned.

i.  **Verify Delivery of Objectives:** Confirm that the project has achieved its defined objectives and delivered the required outputs [Ref 12].
ii. **Obtain Formal Acceptance:** Secure formal acceptance of the final product, service, or result from the customer or sponsor.
iii. **Close Procurements:** Formally close all project contracts.
iv. **Conduct Lessons Learned:** Document successes, challenges, and recommendations for future projects. This contributes to organizational learning.
v. **Release Resources:** Formally release project team members and other project resources.
vi. **Formal Project Closure:** Administer the final project closure activities, including archiving project documents and communicating project closure to all stakeholders [Ref 6, Ref 12].

*   **Key Stakeholders:** Individuals, groups, or organizations affected by the project, including end-users, subject matter experts, and operational staff [Ref 7]. Their effective engagement is crucial [Ref 15].

### Decision Gates

Decision gates are critical control points throughout the project lifecycle, ensuring that the project remains aligned with strategic objectives and is ready to proceed to the next phase. Project governance principles [Ref 14] dictate these checkpoints.

i.  **Gate 1: Project Authorization (End of Initiation Phase)**
    *   **Purpose:** To formally approve the project charter, confirming that the project is strategically aligned, feasible, and adequately resourced to begin detailed planning. This decision confirms the project's authority to move forward [Ref 14].
    *   **Decision Makers:** Project Sponsor, key executive management.

ii. **Gate 2: Project Plan Approval (End of Planning Phase)**
    *   **Purpose:** To approve the comprehensive project plan, including detailed scope, schedule, budget, resource, and risk management plans [Ref 2]. This ensures all stakeholders agree on the approach before significant execution begins.
    *   **Decision Makers:** Project Sponsor, Project Manager, key functional managers.

iii. **Gate 3: Phase Completion Reviews (During Execution Phase)**
    *   **Purpose:** For iterative or incremental development approaches, these gates occur at the end of each major development iteration or stage. They review completed deliverables, assess progress against the baseline, and decide on adjustments for subsequent phases [Ref 12]. These gates ensure "good results within the context of project management" [Ref 13].
    *   **Decision Makers:** Project Manager, Project Sponsor, Development Team Lead, QA Lead, Business Analyst.

iv. **Gate 4: Go-Live Approval (Pre-Deployment)**
    *   **Purpose:** To assess the readiness of the software for deployment to a production environment. This includes reviewing final testing results, operational readiness, and user acceptance.
    *   **Decision Makers:** Project Sponsor, Project Manager, QA Lead, Operational Stakeholders, Key Business Stakeholders.

v.  **Gate 5: Project Final Acceptance (End of Closure Phase)**
    *   **Purpose:** To formally acknowledge that all project objectives have been met, deliverables accepted, and the project is complete [Ref 12]. This signifies the transfer of project outcomes to operations and the dissolution of the project team [Ref 6].
    *   **Decision Makers:** Project Sponsor, Customer/Key Business Stakeholder.
