### Phases and Activities

The project lifecycle will follow a structured approach, integrating PRINCE2 processes into distinct management phases, streamlined for a short project duration.

#### I. Starting Up a Project (SU) / Initiation
This phase establishes the project's foundation, ensuring a legitimate and viable start.

i.  **Appoint Project Executive & Project Manager:** Formal appointment of key leadership roles. The Project Manager is responsible for applying methods, tools, and techniques to meet objectives [Ref 4].
ii. **Capture Project Mandate:** Confirm the initial trigger for the project and its alignment with organizational strategy [Ref 15].
iii. **Define Project Brief:** Create a high-level project definition, outlining objectives, scope, expected outcomes, and initial business justification. This includes identifying initial stakeholders who have interests in or can be affected by the project [Ref 8].
iv. **Identify Stakeholders:** Conduct an initial analysis to identify all relevant stakeholders and their interests, a critical performance domain [Ref 2, Ref 3].
v. **Prepare Outline Business Case:** Develop an initial justification for the project, focusing on the value it will deliver [Ref 12].
vi. **Conduct Feasibility Review:** Assess the project's viability and desirability before committing significant resources to planning.

#### II. Initiating a Project (IP) / Planning
This phase focuses on detailed planning and establishing project controls.

i.  **Develop Project Initiation Documentation (PID):** Create the foundational project document, incorporating detailed project scope (product and project), objectives, and a comprehensive plan [Ref 1].
ii. **Detailed Scope Definition:** Refine product and project scope, breaking down deliverables into a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) and work packages [Ref 1, Ref 3]. Deliverables must be unique and verifiable elements [Ref 7].
iii. **Estimating & Scheduling:** Develop detailed estimates for effort, cost, and duration, leading to a comprehensive project schedule [Ref 1].
iv. **Budget Allocation:** Secure and allocate the necessary financial resources [Ref 1].
v. **Team Composition & Roles:** Define the project team structure and individual roles and responsibilities [Ref 1].
vi. **Communication Plan:** Establish how and when information will be shared with stakeholders [Ref 1].
vii. **Procurement Strategy:** Outline how external resources or services will be acquired, if necessary [Ref 1].
viii. **Risk Management Strategy:** Identify potential threats and opportunities, and plan for their management [Ref 3, Ref 14]. This includes optimizing risk responses [Ref 12].
ix. **Change Management Strategy:** Define the process for managing and approving changes to the project baseline [Ref 1].
x. **Quality Management Strategy:** Detail how quality will be built into processes and deliverables [Ref 12].
xi. **Tailoring PRINCE2:** Adapt the standard PRINCE2 processes, roles, and management products to fit the specific context of this small, IT construction project [Ref 12].

#### III. Directing a Project (DP) / Managing a Stage Boundary (SB) / Controlling a Stage (CS) / Managing Product Delivery (MP) / Execution & Monitoring
This phase involves executing the plans, producing deliverables, and maintaining control. For a small project, these might be grouped into 1-2 stages.

i.  **Authorize a Stage or Project:** The Project Board authorizes the Project Manager to proceed with a stage or the entire project.
ii. **Manage Product Delivery:** Project Teams carry out the work, producing the specialized products as defined in the work packages [Ref 3, Ref 7].
iii. **Monitor & Control Progress:** Regularly track progress against the plan, measuring performance and reporting on deviations [Ref 1, Ref 2].
iv. **Issue & Change Control:** Manage unexpected events (issues) and formal requests for changes [Ref 7]. This involves enabling change to achieve the envisioned future state [Ref 12].
v. **Risk Management:** Continuously monitor and respond to identified risks and new risks [Ref 12].
vi. **Quality Assurance:** Ensure deliverables meet defined quality criteria.
vii. **Stakeholder Engagement:** Maintain effective engagement with stakeholders, providing regular updates and managing expectations [Ref 2, Ref 11, Ref 12].
viii. **Highlight Reports:** Project Manager provides regular progress reports to the Project Board.
ix. **Stage Boundary Reviews:** At predefined points (e.g., mid-project for a short project, or end of significant work packages), review the current stage, update the business case, and plan the next steps, including tailoring [Ref 12].

#### IV. Closing a Project (CP) / Closure
This phase formally wraps up the project.

i.  **Decommission Project:** Verify that all project deliverables are complete and accepted by the Senior User.
ii. **Handover Products:** Formally transfer the project's products to operations or the client.
iii. **Evaluate Project Performance:** Assess the project against its original objectives and business case. This is part of the measurement performance domain [Ref 2].
iv. **Capture Lessons Learned:** Document successes, failures, and recommendations for future projects.
v. **Release Resources:** Formally release project team members and other resources.
vi. **Formal Project Closure:** Obtain final approval for project closure from the Project Board.

### Roles

The following key project roles are essential for successful project delivery, aligning with PRINCE2 and broader PM standards [Ref 4, Ref 8, Ref 9]:

i.  **Project Executive (Sponsor):** Accountable for the project's overall success, providing resources and executive decisions [Ref 8]. Represents business interests.
ii. **Senior User:** Represents the interests of those who will use the project's products or who will be impacted by them. Ensures the solution is fit for purpose.
iii. **Senior Supplier:** Represents the interests of those who provide the specialist products, resources, or services for the project. Ensures the technical viability of the solution.
iv. **Project Manager:** Responsible for the day-to-day management of the project, ensuring it meets its objectives. This includes planning, executing, and monitoring [Ref 4, Ref 1].
v. **Project Team Members:** Individuals who carry out the work to deliver the project's products.
vi. **Project Assurance:** Monitors the project on behalf of the Project Executive, Senior User, and Senior Supplier, ensuring adherence to standards and best practices.
vii. **Project Support:** Provides administrative and coordination services to the Project Manager and Project Board.

### Decision Gates

Decision Gates are critical checkpoints where the Project Board (chaired by the Project Executive) reviews project progress and viability, authorizing continuation or cessation. These gates align with PRINCE2's management by exception and management by stages principles, ensuring the project remains justified [Ref 15].

i.  **End of Starting Up (Go/No-Go for Initiation):** After the Project Brief and outline Business Case are prepared, the Project Board reviews whether to commit resources to detailed planning. This decision considers the initial justification for the project [Ref 15].
ii. **End of Initiating (Approval of PID, Go/No-Go for Execution):** Upon completion of the PID and detailed project plan, the Project Board must formally approve it, confirming the project's viability and committing resources for execution. This gate ensures that the project has a sound basis for its objectives, scope, and resources [Ref 1].
iii. **Stage Boundary Review (Go/No-Go for Next Stage):** For a small, sub-6-month project, this might occur once, typically at a significant midpoint or after a major deliverable is completed. The Project Board reviews the completed stage, assesses the updated Business Case, and authorizes continuation to the next stage of delivery [Ref 6, Ref 15]. This ensures continuous justification and control.
iv. **End of Project (Acceptance and Formal Closure):** The final decision gate where the Project Board confirms that all products have been delivered and accepted, and the project can be formally closed, transferring ownership and responsibilities [Ref 6].
