### Phases and Activities

The project will follow a phased approach, providing structured control points while allowing for iterative elements inherent in research. Project management should be performed through a set of processes to achieve objectives [Ref 13].

#### I. Project Initiation
This phase establishes the foundational elements and formal authorization for the project.
*   i. **Define Project Objectives and Scope:** Clearly articulate the research objectives and the initial product scope, understanding that scope may evolve in research but needs a baseline [Ref 1, Ref 6].
*   ii. **Identify and Engage Stakeholders:** Identify all persons, groups, or organizations that have interests in, or can affect or be affected by, the project [Ref 5, Ref 6]. Effective engagement is crucial [Ref 15].
*   iii. **Develop Project Charter:** Document the project's purpose, objectives, initial scope, key deliverables [Ref 10], high-level requirements, and assigned Project Manager, formally authorizing the project. This aligns with establishing initial project governance [Ref 7].
*   iv. **Appoint Project Sponsor and Manager:** Formalize the roles, noting the Sponsor's responsibility for resources and executive decisions [Ref 6].
*   v. **Preliminary Risk Assessment:** Identify initial threats and opportunities that could impact objectives [Ref 4, Ref 14].

#### II. Project Planning
This phase details how the project objectives will be achieved, building quality into processes and deliverables [Ref 15]. This is where product scope, project scope, estimating, scheduling, budget, team composition, communication, procurement, change management, and measurement plans are defined [Ref 1].
*   i. **Develop Project Management Plan:** A comprehensive document integrating all subsidiary plans.
*   ii. **Define Scope and Create Work Breakdown Structure (WBS):** Decompose the defined scope into progressively lower levels of work elements [Ref 4], leading to defined work packages [Ref 4]. This includes both product scope (what the research delivers) and project scope (the work to deliver it) [Ref 1].
*   iii. **Estimate, Schedule, and Budget:** Develop detailed estimates for activities, resources, and costs, creating a comprehensive schedule and budget [Ref 1].
*   iv. **Plan Resources:** Determine team composition [Ref 1], allocate human, material, equipment, and financial resources [Ref 13].
*   v. **Develop Communication Plan:** Define how information will be shared among stakeholders and the team [Ref 1].
*   vi. **Develop Risk Management Plan:** Detail processes for identifying, analyzing, and planning responses to risks, including threats and opportunities [Ref 4, Ref 14]. Optimize risk responses [Ref 15].
*   vii. **Establish Change Management Plan:** Define the process for requesting, evaluating, and approving changes to the project baseline [Ref 1].
*   viii. **Develop Quality Management Plan:** Outline how quality will be ensured throughout the project, aligning with building quality into processes and deliverables [Ref 15].
*   ix. **Plan for Measurement and Performance:** Define metrics and processes for measuring project and research performance [Ref 1, Ref 5].

#### III. Project Execution (Research & Development)
This phase involves performing the actual work of the project to achieve the objectives [Ref 5].
*   i. **Direct and Manage Project Work:** Execute the planned activities, manage resources, and motivate individuals involved to achieve project objectives [Ref 13].
*   ii. **Conduct Research Activities:** Perform experiments, data collection, analysis, and iterative development cycles characteristic of an IT research project.
*   iii. **Manage Team:** Foster a collaborative environment [Ref 15], assign tasks, and facilitate team development [Ref 1].
*   iv. **Manage Communications:** Implement the communication plan, engaging effectively with stakeholders [Ref 15].
*   v. **Manage Procurements:** Oversee any external contracts or purchases [Ref 1].
*   vi. **Develop Deliverables:** Produce the unique and verifiable elements required by the project [Ref 10].

#### IV. Project Monitoring & Controlling
This phase involves tracking, reviewing, and regulating the progress and performance of the project to identify areas where changes to the plan are required. Measurement is a key performance domain [Ref 5].
*   i. **Monitor and Control Project Work:** Track progress against the plan, measuring actual performance [Ref 1].
*   ii. **Manage Scope Changes:** Review and approve changes to the scope, preventing scope creep, as ambiguity regarding deliverables can lead to unrealistic expectations [Ref 3, Ref 1].
*   iii. **Monitor Risks:** Continuously identify new risks and monitor existing ones, implementing planned risk responses [Ref 4, Ref 14, Ref 15].
*   iv. **Manage Issues:** Address any events that arise during the project that require management attention [Ref 10].
*   v. **Control Quality:** Monitor specific project results to determine if they comply with relevant quality standards and identify ways to eliminate causes of unsatisfactory performance.
*   vi. **Monitor and Control Communications:** Ensure effective information flow.
*   vii. **Monitor and Control Procurement:** Ensure vendor performance meets contractual obligations.

#### V. Project Closure
This phase formally completes all activities across all process groups to formally close the project or phase [Ref 9].
*   i. **Formalize Project Completion:** Obtain final acceptance of all research deliverables [Ref 10].
*   ii. **Conduct Post-Mortem/Lessons Learned:** Analyze project successes and failures, capturing knowledge for future projects.
*   iii. **Release Resources:** Formally release project team members and other resources.
*   iv. **Archive Project Documents:** Compile and store all project documentation for future reference.
*   v. **Close Contracts:** Formally close all procurement contracts.

### Roles

Key roles are essential for accountability and clear decision-making, addressing potential ambiguity regarding responsibilities [Ref 3].

*   **Project Sponsor:** Person responsible for obtaining resources and executive decisions to enable project success [Ref 6]. Provides strategic direction and removes roadblocks.
*   **Project Manager:** Responsible for coordinated activities to direct and control the accomplishment of agreed objectives [Ref 6]. Integrates practices to initiate, plan, execute, monitor, control, and close the project, managing resources and motivating individuals [Ref 13].
*   **Research Lead:** Drives the technical aspects of the research, guiding the research team and ensuring scientific rigor.
*   **Project Team Members:** Individuals performing the work to achieve project objectives [Ref 5, Ref 11]. In a research project, this includes researchers, developers, data scientists, etc.
*   **Stakeholders:** Any person, group, or organization that has interests in, or can affect, be affected by, or perceive itself to be affected by, any aspect of a project [Ref 6]. This includes internal and external parties.
*   **Quality Assurance (QA) Specialist/Team:** Ensures that the research processes and outputs meet defined quality standards and requirements [Ref 15].

### Decision Gates

Decision gates are critical points where key stakeholders formally review the project's progress and performance, ensuring authorization and direction to accomplish agreed objectives [Ref 7, Ref 10]. These gates help manage uncertainty and complexity inherent in large research projects [Ref 15].

*   **I. Project Charter Approval Gate:**
    *   **Purpose:** To formally authorize the project and commit resources based on the defined objectives, initial scope, and high-level plan.
    *   **Decision Authority:** Project Sponsor, Executive Management [Ref 6].
    *   **Evidence Required:** Approved Project Charter, Preliminary Business Case, High-level Risk Assessment.
*   **II. Planning Completion / Baseline Approval Gate:**
    *   **Purpose:** To approve the detailed Project Management Plan, including scope, schedule, budget, and resource plans, establishing the project baseline.
    *   **Decision Authority:** Project Sponsor, Project Manager, Key Stakeholders.
    *   **Evidence Required:** Approved Project Management Plan (including WBS, Schedule, Budget, Risk, Communication, Quality, and Change Management Plans) [Ref 1, Ref 4, Ref 13].
*   **III. Mid-Phase Review Gates (Iterative for Research Stages):**
    *   **Purpose:** For a large research project, these gates occur at logical breaks (e.g., after a major research experiment, prototype completion, or data analysis milestone). They assess progress, validate research direction, review interim deliverables [Ref 10], and allow for course correction.
    *   **Decision Authority:** Project Sponsor, Project Manager, Research Lead.
    *   **Evidence Required:** Progress Reports, Interim Research Findings, Risk Register Updates, Change Requests, Quality Metrics [Ref 1, Ref 5]. This supports the "measurement" performance domain [Ref 5].
*   **IV. Final Deliverable Acceptance Gate:**
    *   **Purpose:** To confirm that all primary research deliverables [Ref 10] and objectives have been met and are acceptable to the sponsor and key stakeholders.
    *   **Decision Authority:** Project Sponsor, Key Stakeholders.
    *   **Evidence Required:** Completed Research Report, Validated Prototypes/Solutions, Final Data Sets, Quality Assurance Reports [Ref 10].
*   **V. Project Closure Gate:**
    *   **Purpose:** To formally close the project, confirm administrative closure, and approve the transfer of deliverables to operations or follow-on projects.
    *   **Decision Authority:** Project Sponsor.
    *   **Evidence Required:** Lessons Learned Report, Final Performance Report, Resource Release Confirmations, Contract Closure Notifications [Ref 9].
