### Phases and Activities

The project will be structured into five distinct phases, moving sequentially through the project lifecycle from initiation to closure [Ref 6, Ref 8].

#### I. Initiating Phase
This phase formally defines a new project or a new phase by obtaining authorization to start [Ref 8].

i.  **Develop Project Charter**: Define the project's high-level objectives, initial scope, and assign the Project Manager.
ii. **Identify Stakeholders**: Systematically identify all persons, groups, or organizations that have interests in, or can affect, be affected by, or perceive themselves to be affected by, any aspect of the project [Ref 5, Ref 9]. This aligns with the Stakeholder Performance Domain [Ref 1].
iii. **Establish Project Governance**: Define the principles, policies, and procedures by which the project will be authorized and directed to accomplish agreed objectives [Ref 7, Ref 8].
iv. **Conduct Feasibility Study (if required)**: Assess the viability of the research objectives within the construction context.
v.  **Secure Project Authorization**: Obtain formal approval from the Project Sponsor or governing body to proceed to the Planning Phase [Ref 8].

#### II. Planning Phase
This phase establishes the total scope of the project, defines the objectives, and defines the course of action required to attain the objectives [Ref 2]. This is crucial for a large project to navigate complexity [Ref 11].

i.  **Develop Project Management Plan**: Create a comprehensive document integrating all subsidiary plans [Ref 2].
ii. **Define Product and Project Scope**: Clearly articulate what the research project will deliver (product scope) and the work required to deliver it (project scope) [Ref 2]. Develop a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) to decompose the defined scope into progressively lower levels of work elements [Ref 5].
iii. **Estimate Costs and Schedule**: Detail activity durations, resource requirements, and cost estimates, then develop the project schedule and budget [Ref 2].
iv. **Plan Resources and Team Composition**: Determine necessary team competencies and roles, aligning with the Team Performance Domain [Ref 1, Ref 2].
v.  **Develop Communication Management Plan**: Define how, when, and by whom project information will be communicated, ensuring effective engagement with stakeholders [Ref 2, Ref 11].
vi. **Plan Procurement Management**: Identify research materials, services, or expertise to be acquired from outside the project team and plan the procurement process [Ref 2].
vii. **Plan Risk Management**: Identify potential threats and opportunities, analyze their impact, and plan optimal responses to uncertainty [Ref 1 (Uncertainty Performance Domain), Ref 11].
viii. **Establish Change Management Process**: Define procedures for managing changes to the project baseline, including a formal change control board [Ref 2].
ix. **Define Measurement Strategy**: Establish how project performance will be measured, monitored, and controlled across all performance domains [Ref 1 (Measurement Performance Domain), Ref 2].
x.  **Plan Quality Management**: Build quality into processes and deliverables for the research outcomes [Ref 11].

#### III. Executing Phase
This phase involves performing the work defined in the Project Management Plan to achieve project objectives [Ref 1 (Project Work Performance Domain), Ref 9].

i.  **Direct and Manage Project Work**: Lead and perform the activities defined in the project plan, including conducting the actual research in the construction domain [Ref 1 (Project Work Performance Domain)].

iii. **Conduct Procurements**: Manage supplier relationships and ensure timely delivery of procured goods and services.

v.  **Implement Quality Assurance**: Perform planned quality activities to ensure processes are followed and deliverables meet quality standards [Ref 11].

#### IV. Monitoring & Controlling Phase
This phase tracks, reviews, and regulates the progress and performance of the project, identifying any areas in which changes to the plan are required and initiating those changes [Ref 1 (Measurement Performance Domain)].

i.  **Monitor Project Work**: Track actual progress against the planned schedule and budget, and evaluate research performance [Ref 1 (Measurement Performance Domain)].
ii. **Control Scope, Schedule, and Costs**: Manage changes to the project scope, schedule, and cost baselines using the established change management process [Ref 2 (Change management)].
iii. **Monitor and Control Risks**: Continuously identify new risks, re-evaluate existing ones, and implement risk responses, optimizing them as needed [Ref 1 (Uncertainty Performance Domain), Ref 11].
iv. **Perform Quality Control**: Monitor specific project results to determine if they comply with relevant quality standards and identify ways to eliminate causes of unsatisfactory performance [Ref 11].
v.  **Report Performance**: Generate and distribute performance reports to stakeholders, providing insights into progress, issues, and forecasts [Ref 1 (Measurement Performance Domain)].

#### V. Closing Phase
This phase formally completes all activities across all Process Groups to formally close the project or phase [Ref 6].

i.  **Close Project or Phase**: Finalize all activities to formally conclude the project.

v.  **Release Project Resources**: Formally release project team members and other resources.

### Decision Gates

Decision gates are critical points in the project lifecycle where project performance and progress are reviewed by relevant stakeholders and senior management to decide whether to continue the project, modify it, or terminate it. These gates reinforce project governance and control [Ref 8].

i.  **Gate 1: Project Charter Approval**:
    *   **Purpose**: To obtain formal authorization to proceed with detailed planning.
    *   **Decision**: Go/No-Go to Planning Phase.
    *   **Evidence**: Project Sponsor's approval of the Project Charter, signifying project authorization and commitment of resources [Ref 8, Ref 9].
ii. **Gate 2: Project Management Plan Approval**:
    *   **Purpose**: To ensure that a comprehensive and agreed-upon plan is in place before significant execution begins.
    *   **Decision**: Go/No-Go to Executing Phase.
    *   **Evidence**: Formal approval of the integrated Project Management Plan by the Project Sponsor and key stakeholders, confirming alignment on scope, schedule, budget, and risk strategies [Ref 2, Ref 8].
iii. **Gate 3: Mid-Term Research Review / Phase Completion Review (if applicable)**:
    *   **Purpose**: For long projects, periodic reviews are essential to ensure the project remains on track and objectives are still valid. For multi-phase projects, this signifies completion of a major phase.
    *   **Decision**: Continue/Adjust/Terminate.
    *   **Evidence**: Performance reports, research findings to date, and updated forecasts are reviewed against initial objectives and the current plan [Ref 1 (Measurement Performance Domain), Ref 8 (Project life cycle defined set of phases)].
iv. **Gate 4: Final Deliverable Acceptance**:
    *   **Purpose**: To confirm that the project's primary output (research findings, report, prototype) meets the defined requirements and is formally accepted.
    *   **Decision**: Go/No-Go to Project Closure.
    *   **Evidence**: Formal sign-off on the unique and verifiable research deliverables by the Project Sponsor and relevant stakeholders, confirming they meet criteria [Ref 7].
v.  **Gate 5: Project Closure Approval**:
    *   **Purpose**: To formally close the project and acknowledge its completion.
    *   **Decision**: Project Officially Closed.
    *   **Evidence**: Confirmation that all contractual obligations are met, lessons learned documented, and resources released, with formal sign-off by the Project Sponsor [Ref 6].
