### Phases and Activities

The project lifecycle will be structured around PRINCE2's seven processes, adapted to reflect distinct phases of project delivery, ensuring comprehensive coverage from initiation to closure.

#### I. Starting Up a Project (Pre-project Phase)

This initial phase focuses on establishing a solid foundation for the project, ensuring there's a legitimate reason to proceed and that key roles are appointed.
i.  Appoint the Executive and Project Manager: The Project Board Executive and Project Manager are appointed [Ref 6, Ref 12].
ii. Capture Previous Lessons: Review lessons from similar projects or previous engagements.
iii. Design and Appoint the Project Management Team: Define and appoint other project management roles.
iv. Prepare the Outline Business Case: Document the justification for the project, including high-level costs, benefits, and timescales.
v.  Select the Project Approach and Assemble the Project Brief: Define the strategy for project delivery and create the Project Brief document.
vi. Plan the Initiation Stage: Detail the activities, resources, and timeline for the subsequent Initiation Stage [Ref 3].

#### II. Directing a Project (Overall Governance)

This process runs throughout the project, providing overall direction and control from the Project Board.
i.  Authorize Initiation: The Project Board reviews the Project Brief and Initiation Stage Plan to decide whether to authorize the project's initiation.
ii. Authorize the Project: Upon review of the Project Initiation Document (PID), the Project Board authorizes the full project to proceed.
iii. Authorize a Stage or Exception Plan: At the end of each stage, the Project Board reviews progress and authorizes the next stage or approves an exception plan.
iv. Give Ad Hoc Direction: Provide ongoing guidance and make decisions on issues and risks [Ref 11].
v.  Authorize Project Closure: Approve the formal closure of the project after verifying completion of objectives.

#### III. Initiating a Project (Planning Phase)

This phase involves detailed planning to establish the project's baseline and define its objectives, scope, and approach.
i.  Agree on the Project Context and Definition: Formalize the project's purpose and align with organizational objectives [Ref 6].
ii. Develop the Project Plan: Detail project scope, schedule, budget, quality, and resources [Ref 3]. This includes product scope, project scope, estimating, scheduling, budget, and team composition.
iii. Create the Communication Management Strategy: Define how, when, and to whom project information will be communicated [Ref 3, Ref 8].
iv. Develop the Risk Management Strategy: Establish the approach to identifying, assessing, and responding to risks and threats [Ref 3, Ref 4, Ref 10]. In finance infrastructure, this is critical for compliance and system stability.
v.  Formulate the Quality Management Strategy: Define quality standards and assurance processes, essential for finance infrastructure reliability.
vi. Create the Change Control Approach: Establish procedures for managing and controlling changes to the project baseline [Ref 3].
vii. Assemble the Project Initiation Document (PID): Consolidate all planning documents into a comprehensive PID.

#### IV. Controlling a Stage (Execution & Monitoring Phase)

This process focuses on the Project Manager's day-to-day management of a stage, ensuring products are delivered to specification.
i.  Authorize Work Package: Assign work packages to Team Managers [Ref 4].
ii. Review Work Package Status: Monitor progress against plans and track actuals [Ref 1].
iii. Capture and Examine Issues and Risks: Proactively identify, analyze, and manage issues and risks, escalating where necessary [Ref 10, Ref 11].
iv. Report Highlights: Provide regular progress updates to the Project Board.
v.  Take Corrective Action: Implement responses to deviations from the plan, issues, and risks.
vi. Conduct Quality Reviews: Ensure deliverables meet defined quality criteria.

#### V. Managing Product Delivery (Team Execution Phase)

This process governs the agreement, execution, and delivery of work packages between the Project Manager and Team Managers.
i.  Accept Work Package: Team Manager accepts the work package from the Project Manager.
ii. Execute Work Package: Perform the defined activities to create specialist products.
iii. Report Checkpoint: Team Manager provides regular progress reports to the Project Manager.
iv. Deliver Work Package: Submit completed and quality-checked products to the Project Manager.

#### VI. Managing a Stage Boundary (Review & Next Stage Planning)

This process occurs at the end of each management stage, enabling the Project Board to review the stage, approve the next, and review the Project Plan.
i.  Report Stage End: The Project Manager reports the completed stage to the Project Board.
ii. Update the Project Plan: Revise the overall Project Plan based on actual progress and forecasts.
iii. Update the Business Case: Re-evaluate the project's ongoing viability against its original justification.
iv. Plan the Next Stage: Detail the activities, resources, and timeline for the upcoming stage.
v.  Prepare an Exception Plan (if needed): If the stage deviated significantly from plan, propose an exception plan for Project Board approval.

#### VII. Closing a Project (Post-delivery Phase)

This final phase focuses on formal project closure, ensuring all objectives are met and lessons are captured.
i.  Prepare Planned Closure: Verify that all products have been delivered and accepted.
ii. Prepare Premature Closure (if needed): Manage early termination if the project is no longer viable.
iii. Hand Over Products: Formally transfer project deliverables to operations or the client.
iv. Evaluate the Project: Assess project performance against objectives, budget, and schedule [Ref 1].
v.  Recommend Project Closure: The Project Manager recommends formal project closure to the Project Board.
vi. Disband the Project Team: Release project resources.
vii. Capture Lessons Learned: Document project successes, failures, and recommendations for future projects [Ref 13].

### Roles

Key project roles for this PRINCE2-driven finance infrastructure project include:

i.  **Executive**: Accountable for the business case and overall project success; represents the business perspective. Owns the benefits realization.
ii. **Senior User**: Represents the interests of those who will use the project's products. Ensures the solution is fit for purpose.
iii. **Senior Supplier**: Represents the interests of those who provide the specialist products and resources required for the project.
iv. **Project Manager**: Manages the project on a day-to-day basis within the tolerances set by the Project Board [Ref 5, Ref 6]. Responsible for planning [Ref 3], project work [Ref 1], and delivery [Ref 1].
v.  **Team Manager**: Responsible for the production of specialist products allocated by the Project Manager (if appointed).
vi. **Project Assurance**: Independent role providing assurance to the Project Board regarding the project's adherence to standards and strategies.
vii. **Change Authority**: Group or individual responsible for reviewing and approving/rejecting change requests.
viii. **Project Support**: Provides administrative and coordination services to the Project Manager and project team.
ix. **Stakeholder**: Any person, group, or organization that has an interest in, or can affect, be affected by, or perceive itself to be affected by, any aspect of the project [Ref 1, Ref 4, Ref 6]. Effective engagement with stakeholders is a key principle [Ref 8, Ref 13].
x.  **Sponsor**: Person responsible for obtaining resources and executive decisions [Ref 6]. (Often the Executive in PRINCE2).

### Decision Gates

Decision gates are crucial control points, particularly important in a finance infrastructure project to manage risks and ensure continued business justification. These gates align with PRINCE2's principle of 'manage by stages' and 'continued business justification'. Project governance principles, policies, and procedures ensure these gates are authorized and directed to accomplish agreed objectives [Ref 12].

i.  **Project Authorization (End of Starting Up a Project)**: The Project Board decides whether to authorize the Initiation Stage based on the Project Brief and Outline Business Case.
ii. **Initiation Authorization (End of Initiating a Project)**: The Project Board decides whether to authorize the full project, reviewing the detailed Project Initiation Document (PID) and Project Plan. This is a critical point where budget, schedule, and scope baselines are approved [Ref 3].
iii. **Stage Authorization (End of each Management Stage)**: The Project Board reviews the completed stage, assesses the updated Business Case and Project Plan, and decides whether to authorize the next stage or approve an Exception Plan. This ensures continued project viability and control over deliverables [Ref 11].
iv. **Project Closure Authorization (End of Closing a Project)**: The Project Board formally authorizes the closure of the project after verifying all deliverables are handed over, benefits are planned for realization, and lessons learned are documented.
