### Phases and Activities

This project will follow a sequential, phased approach, allowing for clear decision gates and control points.

#### I. Project Initiation
The initial phase focuses on defining the project's purpose, scope, and securing authorization.
i.  **Define Project Objectives:** Clearly articulate what the research project aims to achieve, ensuring alignment with organizational goals. A project is defined as a "temporary endeavour to achieve one or more defined objectives" [Ref 6].
ii. **Identify Key Stakeholders:** Determine individuals, groups, or organizations that have an interest in, or can affect or be affected by, the project [Ref 4, Ref 6]. This includes the research subjects (students, educators), university leadership, and funding bodies. PMBOK emphasizes effectively engaging with stakeholders [Ref 8].
iii. **Appoint Project Sponsor:** Secure a senior individual responsible for obtaining resources and executive decisions [Ref 6].
iv. **Draft Project Charter:** Develop a high-level document outlining the project's purpose, objectives, initial scope, high-level deliverables (e.g., research methodology, data collection plan, final report), and assigned Project Manager.
v.  **Form Initial Project Team:** Identify core research team members and define their initial responsibilities.
vi. **Obtain Project Authorization:** Secure formal approval from the Project Sponsor and relevant governance bodies to proceed to planning. This aligns with project governance principles [Ref 10].

#### II. Project Planning
This phase elaborates on the project charter into a detailed plan for execution, ensuring all aspects are considered before commencing research work.
i.  **Detailed Scope Definition:** Refine the project and product scope, detailing research questions, methodology, data analysis approach, and expected outcomes [Ref 5]. Develop a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) to decompose the scope into manageable work packages [Ref 4].
ii. **Develop Research Plan & Schedule:** Create a detailed schedule outlining research activities, milestones, and timelines, including data collection, analysis, and report writing. This includes estimating and scheduling activities [Ref 5].
iii. **Resource Planning:** Identify and secure all necessary resources, including personnel, equipment, software, and access to data/participants. This covers team composition [Ref 5].
iv. **Budget Development:** Prepare a comprehensive project budget, including personnel costs, materials, travel, and any ethical review fees. This involves detailed budgeting [Ref 5].
v.  **Risk Management Planning:** Identify potential risks (threats) that could impact the project's objectives (e.g., participant recruitment issues, data quality, ethical concerns). Plan for risk responses to optimize outcomes [Ref 4, Ref 11, Ref 8].
vi. **Quality Planning:** Define quality standards for research data, analysis, and deliverables, and plan for quality assurance activities. PMBOK highlights building quality into processes and deliverables [Ref 8].
vii. **Communication Plan:** Establish how information will be shared among the project team, stakeholders, and relevant external parties [Ref 5].
viii. **Ethical Approval Process:** Plan for and initiate the necessary ethical review and approval procedures relevant to education research.

#### III. Project Execution and Monitoring
This phase involves carrying out the planned research activities, collecting data, analyzing findings, and continuously monitoring progress and performance.
i.  **Direct and Manage Project Work:** Implement the research plan, conduct data collection, perform analysis, and draft research findings. This encompasses the "Project Work" domain [Ref 2].
ii. **Manage Project Team:** Guide and support the project team, ensuring effective collaboration and addressing any performance issues.
iii. **Engage Stakeholders:** Regularly communicate with stakeholders, manage expectations, and involve them as needed for feedback or approvals [Ref 8].
iv. **Monitor and Control Project Progress:** Track progress against the schedule and budget. Identify deviations and implement corrective actions. This aligns with PMBOK's "Measurement" domain [Ref 2].
v.  **Manage Deliverable Production:** Oversee the creation of "unique and verifiable elements that are required to be produced by a project" [Ref 12], such as research data sets, analysis reports, and draft publications.
vi. **Manage Risks and Issues:** Continuously identify new risks, monitor existing ones, and manage any issues that arise during the project [Ref 12]. Optimize risk responses [Ref 8].
vii. **Manage Changes:** Control changes to the project scope, schedule, or budget through a formal change management process [Ref 5].

#### IV. Project Closure
The final phase formally concludes the project, ensuring all work is completed, deliverables are accepted, and lessons learned are documented.
i.  **Obtain Final Deliverable Acceptance:** Secure formal acceptance of all research deliverables (e.g., final report, publication draft, recommendations) by the Project Sponsor and relevant stakeholders.
ii. **Release Project Resources:** Formally release project team members and reallocate any remaining resources. PRINCE2 covers the "final act of project closure" [Ref 9].
iii. **Conduct Lessons Learned:** Facilitate a session to identify successes, challenges, and best practices for future projects.
iv. **Archive Project Documentation:** Organize and archive all project documents, data, and records according to organizational policies.
v.  **Formal Project Closure:** Prepare a final project report and obtain formal sign-off from the Project Sponsor, officially closing the project.

### Roles

The following key roles are crucial for the successful delivery of this education research project:

i.  **Project Sponsor:** The person responsible for obtaining the resources and executive decisions to enable project success [Ref 6]. Provides strategic direction and removes roadblocks.
ii. **Research Lead / Project Manager:** The individual responsible for coordinated activities to direct and control the accomplishment of agreed objectives [Ref 6]. Manages the day-to-day operations, team, stakeholders, and overall project delivery [Ref 13, Ref 14]. For a small research project, this role may combine both PM and lead researcher responsibilities.
iii. **Project Team Members (Researchers):** Individuals who perform the actual research work, including data collection, analysis, and report drafting. Project managers and team members benefit from a common basis to understand and conduct project practices [Ref 13].
iv. **Key Stakeholders:** Persons, groups, or organizations that have interests in, or can affect, be affected by, or perceive themselves to be affected by, any aspect of the project [Ref 4, Ref 6]. This often includes academic departments, ethical review boards, and potentially study participants or beneficiaries.

### Decision Gates

Decision gates are critical control points ensuring that the project progresses only when key criteria are met and formal approval is obtained. These gates also provide opportunities for project governance [Ref 10] to exert oversight.

i.  **Project Initiation Gate (Go/No-Go for Planning):**
    *   **Purpose:** To formally authorize the project to proceed into the detailed planning phase.
    *   **Inputs:** Approved Project Charter, identified Project Manager/Research Lead, preliminary resource estimates.
    *   **Decision By:** Project Sponsor [Ref 6], potentially supported by a steering committee.
    *   **Reference:** This aligns with the concept of project governance where projects are "authorized and directed to accomplish agreed objectives" [Ref 10].

ii. **Project Planning Gate (Go/No-Go for Execution):**
    *   **Purpose:** To approve the comprehensive Project Management Plan, including scope, schedule, budget, and risk management.
    *   **Inputs:** Detailed Project Management Plan (WBS, schedule, budget, risk register, communication plan) [Ref 5].
    *   **Decision By:** Project Sponsor [Ref 6] and potentially key stakeholders.
    *   **Reference:** Ensures that a clear "project (3.20) is likely to achieve its objectives" [Ref 10] before significant resources are committed to execution.

iii. **Key Deliverable Acceptance Gates (Throughout Execution):**
    *   **Purpose:** To obtain formal acceptance of significant research outputs or milestones as they are completed (e.g., ethical approval, data collection completion, preliminary analysis results).
    *   **Inputs:** Completed research deliverables (e.g., approved ethical protocol, validated data sets) [Ref 12].
    *   **Decision By:** Research Lead/Project Manager, Stakeholders (e.g., ethical committee), Project Sponsor.
    *   **Reference:** Focuses on the acceptance of "deliverable unique and verifiable element that is required to be produced by a project" [Ref 12].

iv. **Project Closure Gate (Formal Sign-off):**
    *   **Purpose:** To formally conclude the project, confirming all objectives are met and deliverables accepted.
    *   **Inputs:** Final project report, accepted final deliverables, lessons learned documentation, resource release confirmation.
    *   **Decision By:** Project Sponsor [Ref 6].
    *   **Reference:** Marks the "final act of project closure" [Ref 9], ensuring formal completion and transfer of any ongoing responsibilities.
