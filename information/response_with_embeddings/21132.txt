### Phases and Activities

The project will follow a tailored PRINCE2 lifecycle, adapting its seven processes into logical phases to suit the project's small scale and software development nature. This ensures a controlled yet iterative approach, crucial for software delivery.

#### I. Starting Up a Project (SU)
i.  **Appoint Project Board and Project Manager:** Establish the initial project governance structure [Ref 12] by appointing key leadership roles (Executive, Senior User, Senior Supplier, and Project Manager).
ii. **Capture Previous Lessons:** Review relevant project experiences to inform the current project.
iii. **Design and Appoint Project Management Team:** Detail the project's organizational structure and formally appoint team members.
iv. **Prepare Project Brief:** Develop an initial outline of the project, including its objectives and expected outcomes.
v.  **Define Project Approach:** Determine how the project's products will be delivered (e.g., iterative development within stages for software) [Ref 1].
vi. **Plan Initiation Stage:** Create a detailed plan for the next phase.

#### II. Directing a Project (DP)
i.  **Authorize Initiation:** The Project Board reviews the Project Brief and Stage Plan for Initiation and decides whether to authorize the Initiation Stage. This forms a critical decision gate [Ref 12].
ii. **Authorize the Project:** Upon successful completion of the Initiation Stage, the Project Board authorizes the full project based on the Project Initiation Documentation (PID).
iii. **Authorize a Stage or Exception Plan:** The Project Board reviews progress at the end of each management stage and authorizes the next stage plan or an exception plan if significant deviations occur [Ref 7].
iv. **Give Ad-hoc Direction:** The Project Board provides ongoing guidance and makes decisions as required throughout the project.
v.  **Authorize Project Closure:** The Project Board formally approves the project's closure upon verification that project objectives have been met [Ref 7].

#### III. Initiating a Project (IP)
i.  **Tailor PRINCE2 to the Project:** Adapt the PRINCE2 processes and documents to fit the project's size, complexity, and healthcare context [Ref 6].
ii. **Prepare Business Case:** Develop a detailed justification for the project, including costs, benefits, and timescales.
iii. **Define Project Scope and Product Scope:** Clearly articulate what the project will deliver and what is specifically included in the product functionality [Ref 2]. This will involve a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) to decompose the scope [Ref 4].
iv. **Plan the Project:** Develop a comprehensive Project Plan including detailed estimating, scheduling, and budget [Ref 2]. This also includes defining the development approach and life cycle [Ref 3].
v.  **Set up Project Controls:** Establish mechanisms for monitoring and controlling progress, including measurement criteria [Ref 2], [Ref 15].
vi. **Create Project Initiation Documentation (PID):** Compile key management products (Business Case, Project Plan, Quality Management Approach, Risk Management Approach, Communications Management Approach, Change Control Approach) into the PID. This ensures all planning elements are covered [Ref 2].
vii. **Identify and Analyze Stakeholders:** Understand who has interests in, or can affect, or be affected by the project [Ref 3], [Ref 11]. Develop a Communication Plan [Ref 2].
viii. **Establish Risk Management Strategy:** Identify, assess, and plan responses for individual and overall risks (threats and opportunities) throughout the project lifecycle [Ref 8], [Ref 15]. Given the healthcare industry, this is crucial for patient safety and regulatory compliance.
ix. **Build Quality into Processes and Deliverables:** Define quality requirements and how they will be assured, especially critical in healthcare software development [Ref 6].

#### IV. Controlling a Stage (CS) & Managing Product Delivery (MP)
i.  **Authorize Work Package:** The Project Manager authorizes development teams to perform work packages, which are groups of activities with a defined objective [Ref 4].
ii. **Review Work Package Status:** Regularly monitor and compare actual achievements against planned progress [Ref 15].
iii. **Report Progress:** Provide regular updates to the Project Board and relevant stakeholders on stage progress.
iv. **Capture and Examine Issues & Risks:** Continuously identify and assess issues and changes, controlling them against the project baseline [Ref 15]. Optimize risk responses throughout [Ref 8].
v.  **Take Corrective Action:** Implement necessary adjustments to bring the project back on track or capitalize on opportunities.
vi. **Develop and Deliver Products (Iteratively):** This phase focuses on the actual software development, testing, and delivery in iterative cycles (e.g., sprints) within the stage, consistent with adaptive delivery approaches [Ref 1]. Quality is built into these processes and deliverables [Ref 6].

#### V. Managing a Stage Boundary (SB)
i.  **Assess Stage Performance:** Review the current stage's performance against the Stage Plan.
ii. **Update Project Plan and Business Case:** Adjust the overall Project Plan and Business Case based on actual progress and any changes.
iii. **Produce Next Stage Plan:** Develop a detailed plan for the upcoming management stage.
iv. **Report Stage End:** Provide a comprehensive report to the Project Board, including lessons learned and recommendations for the next stage.

#### VI. Closing a Project (CP)
i.  **Prepare Planned Closure:** Conduct activities to confirm that the project has delivered its objectives and that the final products meet quality criteria.
ii. **Hand Over Products:** Ensure that the final software product is formally handed over to operations or the client.
iii. **Evaluate the Project:** Review the project's overall performance against its objectives and the business case, including a post-project review.
iv. **Recommend Project Closure:** The Project Manager recommends formal project closure to the Project Board [Ref 7].
v.  **Disband Project Team:** Formally release project resources.

### Roles

Key project roles, aligned with PRINCE2 principles, are essential for clarity of responsibility and effective governance [Ref 5].

i.  **Executive:** Accountable for the project, ensures it delivers value. Appoints Project Manager.
ii. **Senior User:** Represents the needs of those who will use the project's products. Ensures the solution meets user requirements.
iii. **Senior Supplier:** Represents the interests of those designing, developing, facilitating, and implementing the project's products (e.g., the software development team, external vendors).
iv. **Project Manager (PM):** Manages the project on a day-to-day basis within the constraints set by the Project Board. Responsible for planning, monitoring, and controlling all aspects of the project.
v.  **Team Manager:** Responsible for ensuring the development of products assigned by the Project Manager. Leads the technical team (software developers, testers). For a small project, this role might be combined with the Project Manager or a lead developer.
vi. **Project Assurance:** Monitors the project's performance and compliance on behalf of the Project Board. Can be delegated to an independent person.
vii. **Project Support:** Provides administrative and logistical support to the Project Manager and team. For a small project, this might be a part-time role or handled by the Project Manager.
viii. **Change Authority:** For small projects, this is usually the Project Board or a delegated individual from the Board. Manages and approves changes to the project baseline.

### Decision Gates

Decision gates are crucial control points within the project lifecycle, often driven by the Project Board's authorization responsibilities [Ref 12].

i.  **Project Mandate Acceptance (Pre-Project to Starting Up a Project):** Formal acceptance by the Executive that the initial project idea has merit and can proceed to the 'Starting Up a Project' process. This is the very first step in authorizing the project's existence.
ii. **Initiation Authorization (Starting Up a Project to Initiating a Project):** The Project Board reviews the Project Brief and the Initiation Stage Plan. A key decision point where the Project Board authorizes the Project Manager to proceed with detailed planning [Ref 7].
iii. **Project Authorization (Initiating a Project to First Delivery Stage):** The Project Board reviews and approves the Project Initiation Documentation (PID). This gate officially authorizes the project to proceed beyond planning into the first stage of product delivery, committing significant resources.
iv. **Stage Boundary Approvals (End of a Stage to Next Stage):** At the end of each management stage, the Project Board reviews the completed stage's performance, the updated Project Plan, and the Next Stage Plan. This is a critical point for the Project Board to authorize continuation into the subsequent stage or to halt the project [Ref 7]. This iterative approval ensures ongoing project viability and control.
v.  **Project Closure Authorization (Closing a Project):** The Project Board formally reviews the End Project Report and confirms that all project objectives have been met and products handed over. This gate signifies the formal end of the project and allows for the disbanding of resources [Ref 7].
