### Phases and Activities

The project will follow a sequential phase-gate approach, typical of predictive life cycles within PMBOK, but streamlined for a small project.

#### I. Initiating Phase
This phase formally defines and authorizes the project.
i.  **Identify Project Sponsor and Project Manager:** Formalize leadership roles. The Sponsor is "responsible for obtaining the resources and executive decisions to enable success" [Ref 6].
ii. **Define Project Objectives and High-Level Scope:** Establish clear, measurable objectives for the marketing campaign. A project is a "temporary endeavour to achieve one or more defined objectives" [Ref 6].
iii. **Identify Key Stakeholders:** Recognize all individuals, groups, or organizations "that has interests in, or can affect, be affected by, or perceive itself to be affected by, any aspect of a project" [Ref 4, Ref 6]. This includes internal teams (marketing, finance, legal, compliance) and external groups (e.g., target audience, regulatory bodies). Effective engagement with stakeholders is a core project principle [Ref 9].
iv. **Develop Project Charter:** Document the project's purpose, objectives, high-level requirements, assumptions, constraints, and initial identified risks. This document formally authorizes the project and empowers the Project Manager.
v. **Obtain Project Charter Approval:** Secure formal sign-off from the Project Sponsor.

#### II. Planning Phase
This is a critical phase for a small project, requiring comprehensive definition to ensure successful execution within the tight timeframe. This phase draws heavily from the PMBOK's Planning Performance Domain [Ref 2].
i.  **Detailed Scope Definition:** Refine the "Product scope" (what the marketing campaign will deliver) and "Project scope" (the work required to deliver it). This involves creating a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) by decomposing the defined scope into "progressively lower levels consisting of elements of work" [Ref 3, Ref 4].
ii. **Activity Definition and Sequencing:** Break down work packages into specific activities and determine their logical order.
iii. **Estimating:** Perform "Estimating" for activity durations, resource requirements, and costs [Ref 3].
iv. **Scheduling:** Develop a detailed project schedule based on estimated durations and sequences [Ref 3].
v. **Budget Development:** Create a comprehensive project budget, allocating funds for all planned activities and resources [Ref 3].
vi. **Resource Planning:** Identify required "Team composition" and other resources, including availability and allocation [Ref 3, Ref 12].
vii. **Risk Management Planning:** Conduct a thorough risk identification, analysis, and response planning, optimizing responses to identified risks [Ref 9]. This includes threats (negative impacts) [Ref 4, Ref 7] and opportunities.
viii. **Communication Planning:** Develop a "Communication plan" outlining what information will be communicated, to whom, when, by whom, and how [Ref 3].
ix. **Quality Management Planning:** Define quality standards for marketing deliverables and how they will be achieved and measured. "Build quality into processes and deliverables" is a project principle [Ref 9].
x. **Procurement Planning (if applicable):** If external vendors are required, plan for their selection and management [Ref 3].
xi. **Change Management Planning:** Establish a "Change management" process for handling requests to alter project scope, schedule, or budget [Ref 3].
xii. **Measurement Planning:** Define key performance indicators (KPIs) and metrics for tracking project progress and success, aligning with the "Measurement" performance domain [Ref 2, Ref 3].
xiii. **Tailoring and Context Review:** Ensure the plan considers internal factors like organizational maturity and resource availability, and external factors like regulatory and socio-economic conditions specific to the finance industry [Ref 9, Ref 12, Ref 15].
xiv. **Obtain Project Management Plan Approval:** Secure formal approval of the integrated project plan from the Project Sponsor and key stakeholders.

#### III. Executing Phase
This phase involves carrying out the work defined in the project management plan.
i.  **Direct and Manage Project Work:** Execute the planned marketing activities and produce deliverables, focusing on "Project Work" [Ref 2].
ii. **Acquire and Develop Project Team:** Onboard team members and facilitate their development and collaboration. Create a "collaborative project team environment" [Ref 9].
iii. **Manage Communications:** Implement the communication plan, ensuring timely and effective information flow among stakeholders.
iv. **Conduct Procurements (if applicable):** Manage vendor relationships and contract execution.
v. **Implement Risk Responses:** Take actions to mitigate threats and capitalize on opportunities.
vi. **Manage Stakeholder Engagement:** Continuously engage with stakeholders, managing their expectations and addressing their concerns [Ref 9].

#### IV. Monitoring & Controlling Phase
This phase tracks, reviews, and regulates the progress and performance of the project.
i.  **Monitor and Control Project Work:** Track progress against the plan, using defined metrics and "Measurement" [Ref 2, Ref 3].
ii. **Perform Integrated Change Control:** Review all change requests, approve or reject them, and manage approved changes [Ref 3].
iii. **Monitor and Control Risks:** Continuously identify new risks, re-evaluate existing ones, and implement risk responses as needed [Ref 9].
iv. **Monitor and Control Quality:** Verify that deliverables meet the defined quality standards.
v. **Monitor and Control Communications:** Ensure the communication plan is effective and adjust as necessary.
vi. **Monitor and Control Procurements (if applicable):** Oversee vendor performance and contract compliance.

#### V. Closing Phase
This phase formally concludes all activities across all process groups to formally complete the project.
i.  **Final Product, Service, or Result Transition:** Ensure the marketing campaign deliverables are successfully launched and transitioned to operations or customers [Ref 2, Ref 15].
ii. **Perform Administrative Closure:** Complete all contractual obligations, update organizational process assets, and conduct lessons learned.
iii. **Release Project Team:** Formally release team members and reassign resources.
iv. **Obtain Final Project Acceptance:** Secure formal acceptance of the project deliverables from the customer/sponsor.
v. **Project Closure Report:** Document the project's performance, outcomes, and lessons learned.

### Roles

The following key roles are essential for the project's success, encompassing the "Team" and "Stakeholder" Performance Domains [Ref 2]:

*   **Project Sponsor:** The ultimate authority and champion for the project, providing resources and executive decisions [Ref 6]. Ensures alignment with organizational strategy [Ref 12].
*   **Project Manager:** Responsible for coordinated activities to "direct and control the accomplishment of agreed objectives" [Ref 6]. This includes planning, executing, and closing the project.
*   **Marketing Lead/Specialist:** Responsible for the content, creative, and tactical execution of the marketing campaign.
*   **Finance Subject Matter Expert (SME):** Provides input on budget, financial regulations, and performance metrics relevant to the finance industry.
*   **Legal & Compliance Specialist:** Ensures all marketing materials and activities adhere to industry regulations and company policies, crucial in the finance sector.
*   **Core Project Team Members:** Individuals assigned to execute specific tasks and create deliverables.
*   **Key Stakeholders:** Any "person, group or organization that has interests in, or can affect, be affected by, or perceive itself to be affected by" [Ref 6] the project, including internal departments (e.g., IT, Sales) and external entities.

### Decision Gates

Decision gates are formal points where the project's progress and viability are reviewed, and authorization to proceed to the next phase is granted. These gates ensure appropriate governance and control.

1.  **Project Charter Approval Gate:** At the end of the Initiating Phase, the Project Sponsor formally approves the Project Charter. This decision authorizes the Project Manager to proceed with detailed planning and commits organizational resources to the project.
2.  **Project Management Plan Approval Gate:** At the end of the Planning Phase, the Project Sponsor and key stakeholders formally approve the comprehensive Project Management Plan. This gate ensures that all planning elements (scope, schedule, budget, resources, risks, etc.) are agreed upon before execution begins [Ref 3].
3.  **Go/No-Go for Launch Gate:** During the Monitoring & Controlling Phase, prior to the actual launch of the marketing campaign, a formal review is held. This gate assesses the readiness of deliverables, compliance checks (especially critical in finance), remaining risks, and overall project performance [Ref 2]. The Project Sponsor, with input from Legal & Compliance, will make the final decision to proceed with launch.
4.  **Project Closure Approval Gate:** At the end of the Closing Phase, the Project Sponsor formally signs off on the project closure, confirming that all objectives have been met and deliverables accepted. This gate formally ends the project and releases resources.
