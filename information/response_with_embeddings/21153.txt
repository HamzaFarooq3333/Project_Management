### Phases and Activities

The project will follow a standard lifecycle, adapted to its small scale and ISO requirements, covering the entire project lifecycle from initiation to closure [Ref 9].

#### I. Project Initiation
This phase formally starts the project, defines its purpose, and gains authorization.
i.  **Define Project Objectives and Scope:** Clearly establish the one or more defined objectives of the temporary endeavor [Ref 14]. Document the product scope and initial project scope [Ref 4].
ii. **Identify and Analyze Stakeholders:** Recognize all individuals, groups, or organizations that have interests in, or can affect, or be affected by the project [Ref 3, Ref 6]. Effectively engage with them from the outset [Ref 7].
iii. **Appoint Project Manager and Core Team:** Assign the person responsible for coordinated activities to direct and control the accomplishment of agreed objectives [Ref 6, Ref 13].
iv. **Develop Project Charter:** Obtain authorization from the Sponsor [Ref 6] for the project's existence, outlining objectives, high-level scope, and assigned Project Manager.

#### II. Project Planning
This phase details how the project will be executed, monitored, controlled, and closed. Effective planning is crucial for achieving objectives [Ref 13].
i.  **Develop Project Management Plan:** Integrate practices to direct, initiate, plan, monitor, control, and close the project [Ref 13]. This central document will encompass various subsidiary plans.
ii. **Define Product and Project Scope:** Clearly delineate what the software will deliver (product scope) and the work required to deliver it (project scope) [Ref 4]. Create a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) to decompose the defined scope into progressively lower levels of work elements [Ref 3].
iii. **Estimate Effort, Schedule, and Budget:** Develop realistic estimates for effort, establish a project schedule, and define the project budget [Ref 4].
iv. **Plan for Team Composition and Resource Allocation:** Define team composition and plan for resource allocation, including managing resources assigned to the project [Ref 4, Ref 13].
v.  **Develop Communication Plan:** Outline how communication will be managed with all stakeholders [Ref 4].
vi. **Plan for Risk Management:** Identify potential threats (risk occurrences with negative impact) and opportunities, and optimize risk responses [Ref 3, Ref 7, Ref 10].
vii. **Develop Change Management Plan:** Establish a process for managing changes to the project scope, schedule, and budget [Ref 4].
viii. **Define Quality and Measurement Metrics:** Build quality into processes and deliverables [Ref 7]. Define measurement criteria for tracking project performance [Ref 2, Ref 4].

#### III. Project Execution (Development & Delivery)
This phase involves carrying out the work defined in the planning phase to achieve project objectives.
i.  **Perform Project Work:** Direct the project team to perform the planned work, managing resources and motivating individuals [Ref 2, Ref 13].
ii. **Develop Software Deliverables:** Execute design, coding, and unit testing activities as per the defined product scope.
iii. **Conduct Quality Assurance and Testing:** Implement processes to build quality into deliverables [Ref 7], including integration testing and user acceptance testing (UAT).
iv. **Manage Stakeholder Engagement and Communication:** Continuously engage with stakeholders [Ref 7] and disseminate information as per the communication plan.

#### IV. Project Monitoring & Control
This phase runs concurrently with execution to track progress, manage changes, and ensure objectives are met.
i.  **Monitor Project Performance:** Continuously measure project performance against the plan, including scope, schedule, budget, and quality [Ref 2, Ref 4].
ii. **Manage Risks and Issues:** Monitor identified risks, implement risk responses, and address new threats or issues [Ref 7, Ref 10].
iii. **Control Changes:** Process all change requests through the defined change management plan [Ref 4].
iv. **Report Performance:** Provide regular updates to stakeholders and the Project Sponsor on project status and forecasts.

#### V. Project Closure
This phase formally concludes the project, ensuring all activities are completed and objectives are achieved.
i.  **Verify Scope Completion:** Confirm that all defined deliverables and objectives have been met [Ref 14].
ii. **Obtain Formal Acceptance:** Secure formal acceptance of the final product from the customer/stakeholders.
iii. **Conduct Post-Project Review:** Evaluate project performance against initial objectives, identify lessons learned, and document best practices.
iv. **Release Project Resources:** Formally release the project team and other resources.
v.  **Archive Project Documentation:** Organize and archive all project documents for future reference.
vi. **Formal Project Closure:** Officially close the project, communicating its completion to all stakeholders.

### Roles

Key roles are essential for clear accountability and successful project delivery [Ref 5, Ref 13].

i.  **Project Sponsor:** Person responsible for obtaining resources and executive decisions, enabling project success [Ref 6]. Provides guidance and support to the Project Manager [Ref 11].
ii. **Project Manager:** Responsible for coordinated activities to direct and control the accomplishment of agreed objectives [Ref 6, Ref 13]. Manages resources and motivates individuals involved [Ref 13]. Is the primary point of contact for the project [Ref 15].
iii. **Project Team Members:** Individuals performing the project work, including developers, testers, and business analysts [Ref 2, Ref 7].
iv. **Key Stakeholders:** Persons, groups, or organizations affected by or perceiving themselves to be affected by the project [Ref 3, Ref 6]. This includes end-users, subject matter experts, and organizational management.

### Decision Gates

Decision gates are crucial checkpoints to ensure the project remains aligned with objectives and organizational strategy [Ref 14]. For a small project, these should be frequent but streamlined.

i.  **Project Charter Approval (End of Initiation):** A formal decision by the Project Sponsor to authorize the project and commit initial resources. This ensures a clear understanding of the project's objectives and its alignment with organizational goals before significant planning efforts begin [Ref 6, Ref 14].
ii. **Project Management Plan Approval (End of Planning):** Formal approval by the Project Sponsor (and potentially key stakeholders) of the detailed project plan, including scope, schedule, budget, and risk management strategies [Ref 4, Ref 13]. This gate confirms readiness to proceed with execution.
iii. **Key Deliverable / Milestone Reviews:** Intermediate decision points at the completion of significant project deliverables (e.g., design sign-off, UAT completion). These gates confirm the quality and completeness of work and determine if the project can proceed to the next stage, ensuring objectives are consistently built into deliverables [Ref 7].
iv. **Project Closure Approval (End of Closure):** Formal acceptance by the Project Sponsor and relevant stakeholders that all project objectives have been met, deliverables accepted, and the project can be officially closed [Ref 14]. This is critical for acknowledging successful delivery [Ref 11, Ref 13].
