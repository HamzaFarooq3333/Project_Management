### Process Recommendation: Small-Scale IT Marketing Project (PRINCE2 Tailored)

#### Process Summary

This recommendation outlines a streamlined PRINCE2 process adapted for a small (<6 months) information technology marketing project. Given the project's size and duration, the standard PRINCE2 processes will be tailored for efficiency, focusing on clear roles, defined deliverables, and continuous business justification. Project management, as the application of methods, tools, and techniques to meet objectives [Ref 4], will integrate practices to direct, initiate, plan, monitor, control, and close the project [Ref 12]. The approach will emphasize "tailoring based on context" [Ref 10] and applying principles, practices, and processes "fit for the project context" [Ref 8], as the project's context significantly impacts performance and success [Ref 9]. Key elements such as product scope, project scope, estimating, scheduling, budget, team composition, and communication planning will be central [Ref 1]. Effective stakeholder engagement will be a core principle [Ref 10], ensuring all parties affected by the project are managed appropriately [Ref 7].

-----

#### Phases and Activities

The project lifecycle will be structured into distinct phases, each with specific objectives and activities, aligning with PRINCE2 principles and informed by broader PM standards.

##### I. Pre-Project Activities (Starting Up a Project - SU)

This phase establishes a sound basis for the project before significant resources are committed, ensuring the project is worthwhile and viable.
i.  **Appoint Project Board and Project Manager:** Formalize the Project Executive, Senior User, Senior Supplier, and Project Manager roles [Ref 4], [Ref 7].
ii. **Capture Project Mandate:** Understand the high-level requirement or request for the marketing initiative.
iii. **Identify Stakeholders:** Identify all individuals or groups that have interests in, or can affect or be affected by, the project [Ref 7], [Ref 10].
iv. **Draft Project Brief:** Develop a high-level outline of the project's objectives, scope, and expected benefits, including an initial justification.
v.  **Outline Business Case:** Conduct a preliminary assessment of the project's viability and strategic alignment.
vi. **Identify Initial Risks:** Document initial known threats and opportunities [Ref 14].

##### II. Initiation Phase (Initiating a Project - IP)

This phase aims to establish a solid foundation for the project, detailing how it will be delivered and managed.
i.  **Plan Quality:** Define quality criteria for marketing deliverables (e.g., campaign materials, content).
ii. **Plan the Project:** Develop a comprehensive Project Plan including product scope, project scope, detailed estimating, scheduling, and budget allocation [Ref 1].
iii. **Define Product Descriptions:** Clearly articulate the specific marketing products to be delivered (e.g., website content, social media campaign, email sequences).
iv. **Develop Development Approach:** Determine the specific approach and lifecycle for creating the marketing deliverables (e.g., agile content creation, phased campaign rollout) [Ref 2], [Ref 3].
v.  **Compose Team and Resource Plan:** Outline the project team composition and detailed resource requirements [Ref 1].
vi. **Develop Communication Plan:** Define how, when, and to whom project information will be communicated [Ref 1].
vii. **Risk Management Strategy:** Detail how risks (both threats and opportunities) will be identified, analyzed, and responded to throughout the project [Ref 14].
viii. **Develop Change Management Approach:** Establish procedures for handling changes to the project scope, schedule, or budget [Ref 1].
ix. **Consolidate Project Initiation Documentation (PID):** Compile all plans and strategies into a formal PID for Project Board approval.

##### III. Delivery Stages (Controlling a Stage - CS & Managing Product Delivery - MPD)

This iterative phase focuses on the execution of work packages and the creation of specialist products. For a small project, there might be 1-2 such stages.
i.  **Authorize Work Packages:** Assign specific work packages (groups of activities with defined objectives [Ref 5]) to team members.
ii. **Execute Work Packages:** Team members perform the planned activities to create the marketing deliverables.
iii. **Monitor Progress and Control:** Track actual progress against the stage plan, managing issues, risks, and changes as they arise [Ref 1].
iv. **Quality Check and Review:** Conduct reviews and quality assurance activities for produced deliverables to ensure they meet defined quality criteria.
v.  **Report Progress:** Regularly update the Project Manager and Project Board on stage status and highlight any deviations.
vi. **Manage Stakeholder Engagement:** Continue to effectively engage with stakeholders, providing updates and soliciting feedback as per the communication plan [Ref 10].

##### IV. Stage Boundary Review (Managing a Stage Boundary - SB)

This phase occurs at the end of each delivery stage (or as a mid-project review for very small projects) to review performance and plan the next steps.
i.  **Assess Stage Performance:** Review the completed stage against its plan, identifying lessons learned.
ii. **Update Project Plan:** Revise the overall Project Plan based on actual progress and any changes.
iii. **Update Business Case:** Reconfirm the project's continued business justification and viability.
iv. **Plan Next Stage:** If applicable, create a detailed Stage Plan for the subsequent delivery stage.
v.  **Seek Project Board Approval:** Present the Stage End Report, updated Project Plan, and next Stage Plan (if any) for Project Board approval to proceed.

##### V. Project Closure (Closing a Project - CP)

This phase formally closes the project, confirming acceptance of deliverables and tidying up loose ends.
i.  **Verify Product Acceptance:** Confirm that all marketing deliverables have been accepted by the Senior User.
ii. **Handover Products:** Facilitate the formal handover of marketing assets to operational teams or external clients.
iii. **Evaluate Project Performance:** Review the project against its original objectives and benefits, gathering feedback [Ref 2], [Ref 12].
iv. **Release Resources:** Formally release project team members and other resources.
v.  **Capture Lessons Learned:** Document project successes, challenges, and recommendations for future projects.
vi. **Formal Project Closure:** Prepare a Project Closure Notification for Project Board approval, formally ending the project.

#### Roles

For a small IT marketing project, the PRINCE2 roles will be streamlined to ensure clear accountability without over-burdening individuals.

*   **Executive (Project Board Chair):** Accountable for the project's overall success, ensuring it achieves its objectives and provides value [Ref 7].
*   **Senior User (Project Board Member):** Represents the interests of those who will use the project's products, ensuring the marketing deliverables meet user needs.
*   **Senior Supplier (Project Board Member):** Represents the interests of those designing, developing, and implementing the project's products (e.g., IT department, marketing agency).
*   **Project Manager:** Responsible for the day-to-day management of the project on behalf of the Project Board, ensuring project activities are coordinated and controlled to achieve agreed objectives [Ref 7]. Manages planning, execution, monitoring, and control [Ref 12].
*   **Project Support (Optional/Shared):** Provides administrative and project management support to the Project Manager. For a small project, these duties may be partially absorbed by the Project Manager or another team member.
*   **Team Member(s):** Individuals responsible for executing work packages and producing specialist products as directed by the Project Manager.

#### Decision Gates

Key decision gates are critical points where the Project Board (chaired by the Executive) reviews progress and authorizes the project to proceed. These ensure that the project remains viable and aligned with objectives, emphasizing continued business justification [Ref 8], [Ref 12].

1.  **Project Initiation Authorization:**
    *   **Description:** The Project Board reviews the Project Brief and initial business justification to decide whether to authorize the Initiation Phase.
    *   **Trigger:** Completion of "I. Pre-Project Activities."
    *   **Output:** Project Brief and Project Board authorization to proceed with detailed planning.
    *   **Evidence:** "activities before getting started" [Ref 8].

2.  **Project Authorization:**
    *   **Description:** The Project Board reviews the detailed Project Initiation Documentation (PID), including the Project Plan, Business Case, and strategies, to decide whether to formally authorize the project and its first delivery stage.
    *   **Trigger:** Completion of "II. Initiation Phase."
    *   **Output:** Approved PID, authorization to proceed with "III. Delivery Stage(s)."
    *   **Evidence:** "plan, monitor, control and close the project" [Ref 12]; "the project to meet its objectives" [Ref 4].

3.  **Stage Authorization (if multiple stages):**
    *   **Description:** At the end of each delivery stage, the Project Board reviews the performance of the completed stage and the plan for the next stage (if applicable).
    *   **Trigger:** Completion of a "III. Delivery Stage" and "IV. Stage Boundary Review" activities.
    *   **Output:** Stage End Report, updated Project Plan, and approved Next Stage Plan (or authorization to proceed to closure).
    *   **Evidence:** "through the stages of project delivery" [Ref 8]; "project management integrates the practices included... to direct... and close the project" [Ref 12].

4.  **Project Closure Authorization:**
    *   **Description:** The Project Board reviews the End Project Report and Lessons Learned Report to formally authorize the closure of the project.
    *   **Trigger:** Completion of "V. Project Closure" activities.
    *   **Output:** Formal project closure notification, acceptance of final products.
    *   **Evidence:** "to the final act of project closure" [Ref 8]; "close the project" [Ref 12].
