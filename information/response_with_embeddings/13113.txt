#### 2. Phases and Activities

I. **Initiation Phase:** This phase establishes the project's foundation.

   i. **Project Charter Development:** Define the project's objectives, scope, stakeholders, high-level requirements, and constraints. This step is critical as it sets expectations [Ref 3].
   ii. **Stakeholder Identification and Analysis:** Identify all stakeholders and assess their influence and interests [Ref 4]. This is vital for managing communications and expectations.
   iii. **Project Team Assembly:** Assign project roles and responsibilities.
   iv. **High-Level Risk Assessment:** Identify potential risks and develop mitigation strategies [Ref 1].
   v. **Preliminary Budget and Schedule:** Develop a high-level budget and schedule to estimate feasibility.

II. **Planning Phase:** This phase defines how the project will be executed.

   i. **Detailed Scope Definition:** Break down the project scope into manageable work packages using a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) [Ref 5].
   ii. **Detailed Schedule Development:** Create a realistic schedule, including task dependencies, durations, and resource allocation.
   iii. **Budget Development:** Create a detailed budget.
   iv. **Communication Plan:** Develop a plan to manage communication with stakeholders [Ref 1].
   v. **Procurement Plan (If Applicable):** Define any procurement needs.
   vi. **Risk Management Plan:** Elaborate the risk register and define response plans.
   vii. **Quality Management Plan:** Define quality control measures.
   viii. **Change Management Plan:** Establish a process for managing changes.

III. **Execution Phase:** This phase involves carrying out the project plan.

   i. **Task Execution:** Complete the project's tasks as defined in the plan.
   ii. **Resource Management:** Manage and allocate the resources effectively.
   iii. **Issue Management:** Identify and resolve issues as they arise [Ref 11].
   iv. **Change Request Management:** Follow the change management plan.
   v. **Stakeholder Communication:** Keep stakeholders informed.

IV. **Monitoring and Controlling Phase:** This phase monitors project performance and controls changes.

   i. **Performance Measurement:** Track progress against the baseline schedule, budget, and scope [Ref 4].
   ii. **Variance Analysis:** Identify and analyze any deviations from the plan.
   iii. **Change Control:** Manage change requests according to the change management plan [Ref 1].
   iv. **Risk Monitoring:** Monitor and manage identified risks.
   v. **Status Reporting:** Provide regular project status updates.

V. **Closure Phase:** This phase formally closes the project.

   i. **Deliverable Acceptance:** Obtain formal acceptance of the project deliverables.
   ii. **Project Documentation:** Complete and archive all project documentation.
   iii. **Final Report:** Create a final project report summarizing the project's performance.
   iv. **Lessons Learned:** Conduct a lessons-learned session to identify what went well and what could be improved.
   v. **Contract Closure:** Finalize any contracts [Ref 7].

#### 5. Decision Gates

I. **Project Charter Approval:** At the end of the Initiation Phase, the Project Sponsor approves the Project Charter, formally authorizing the project [Ref 6].
II. **Project Plan Approval:** At the end of the Planning Phase, the Project Manager and the Project Sponsor review and approve the detailed project plan (scope, schedule, budget, etc.).
III. **Change Request Approvals:** Throughout the project, change requests are reviewed and approved by the Change Control Board (or Project Manager, depending on the severity of the change) according to the Change Management Plan.
IV. **Deliverable Acceptance:** Acceptance of deliverables is a key decision gate to ensure quality [Ref 11].
V. **Project Closure Approval:** The Project Sponsor approves the final project report and formally closes the project.
