The framework incorporates key PMBOK performance domains such as Stakeholder, Planning, Project Work, Delivery, Measurement, and Uncertainty [Ref 2], ensuring a holistic management perspective. ### Phases and Activities

#### I. Initiation Phase
This phase formally authorizes the project and establishes its initial scope and objectives.

i.  **Define Project Goals and Objectives**: Clearly articulate what the project aims to achieve and its value proposition.
ii. **Develop Project Charter**: Formally authorize the project, documenting its purpose, high-level objectives, key stakeholders, and assigning the Project Manager. This provides the project manager with the authority to apply organizational resources to project activities [Ref 15].
iii. **Identify Key Stakeholders**: Identify all individuals or groups who have interests in, or can affect, or be affected by, any aspect of the project [Ref 9, Ref 2]. Document their expectations and potential impact.
iv. **Conduct Initial Risk Assessment**: Identify high-level risks, opportunities, and threats that could impact the project [Ref 12].

#### II. Planning Phase
This phase details the scope, schedule, budget, and resources required to achieve project objectives. It is crucial for a predictive approach [Ref 1, Ref 2].

i.  **Define Product and Project Scope**: Clearly define the features and functions of the infrastructure to be delivered (product scope) and the work required to deliver it (project scope) [Ref 1].
ii. **Create Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)**: Decompose the defined scope into progressively lower levels consisting of manageable work elements [Ref 5]. This forms the basis for estimating, scheduling, and budgeting.
iii. **Estimate Activities**: Estimate durations, costs, and resource requirements for each work package [Ref 1].
iv. **Develop Project Schedule**: Sequence activities, assign resources, and establish a realistic timeline for project completion [Ref 1].
v.  **Determine Project Budget**: Consolidate cost estimates to establish the overall project budget [Ref 1].
vi. **Plan for Team Composition and Acquisition**: Define roles and responsibilities and plan for acquiring the necessary team members [Ref 1].
vii. **Develop Communication Management Plan**: Define how, when, and by whom project information will be communicated to stakeholders [Ref 1].
viii. **Develop Risk Management Plan**: Plan for identifying, analyzing, and responding to project risks (opportunities and threats) throughout the project lifecycle [Ref 12]. Document risk responses that are appropriate, cost-effective, and agreed upon by stakeholders [Ref 12].
ix. **Develop Change Management Plan**: Establish processes for managing changes to the project scope, schedule, and budget [Ref 1].
x.  **Plan for Quality and Measurement**: Define quality standards and how project performance and deliverables will be measured and controlled [Ref 1, Ref 2].
xi. **Develop Procurement Plan (if applicable)**: Outline goods or services to be acquired from outside the project team [Ref 1].

#### III. Execution Phase
This phase involves carrying out the project work according to the project plan.

i.  **Direct and Manage Project Work**: Perform the activities defined in the project plan to achieve project objectives [Ref 2].
ii. **Acquire and Develop Project Team**: Onboard and train team members, fostering a collaborative environment [Ref 10].
iii. **Manage Communications**: Implement the communication plan, distributing information to stakeholders as planned.
iv. **Manage Stakeholder Engagement**: Effectively engage with stakeholders, managing expectations and ensuring their continued support [Ref 10].
v.  **Conduct Procurements (if applicable)**: Administer contracts and vendor relationships.

#### IV. Monitoring & Controlling Phase
This phase continuously tracks, reviews, and regulates project progress and performance to identify areas where the plan may need changes. This runs concurrently with the Execution Phase.

i.  **Monitor Project Work**: Track progress against the project schedule and budget.
ii. **Control Scope**: Monitor the status of the project and product scope, managing changes to the scope baseline.
iii. **Control Schedule**: Monitor actual progress against the schedule baseline and manage deviations.
iv. **Control Costs**: Monitor actual expenditures against the budget and manage variances.
v.  **Control Quality**: Monitor specific project results to determine if they comply with relevant quality standards and identify ways to eliminate causes of unsatisfactory performance.
vi. **Manage Risks**: Implement planned risk responses and continuously monitor for new or changing risks [Ref 12].
vii. **Manage Changes**: Review all change requests, approve changes, and manage implementation of approved changes [Ref 1].
viii. **Measure Project Performance**: Collect and analyze performance data to assess project health [Ref 2].

#### V. Closure Phase
This phase formally closes the project or phase.

i.  **Obtain Final Acceptance**: Secure formal acceptance of the project deliverables from the customer or sponsor [Ref 7].
ii. **Perform Administrative Closure**: Close out all contracts, update organizational process assets, and release project resources.
iii. **Conduct Lessons Learned**: Document project successes, failures, and recommendations for future projects.
iv. **Release Project Team**: Formally release team members and acknowledge their contributions.

### Roles

*   **Project Sponsor**: The person responsible for obtaining resources and executive decisions to enable project success [Ref 9]. Accountable for overall project success and strategic alignment.
*   **Project Manager (PM)**: Responsible for the day-to-day management of the project, including planning, executing, monitoring, controlling, and closing. The PM coordinates activities to direct and control the accomplishment of agreed objectives [Ref 9].
*   **IT Infrastructure Lead**: Technical expert responsible for overseeing the design, implementation, and configuration of the infrastructure components.
*   **Technical Team Members**: Individuals responsible for executing specific technical tasks (e.g., network engineers, system administrators).
*   **Key Stakeholders**: Users, operations teams, security teams, and other internal or external parties impacted by or influencing the project.

### Decision Gates

Crucial decision gates ensure project alignment and authorization at key milestones, contributing to effective project governance [Ref 7, Ref 15].

i.  **Project Authorization (End of Initiation)**: Formal approval of the Project Charter by the Project Sponsor, granting authority to the Project Manager to proceed with planning and commit resources. This decision gate ensures the project is formally authorized to accomplish agreed objectives [Ref 15].
ii. **Project Plan Approval (End of Planning)**: Formal approval of the comprehensive Project Management Plan (including scope, schedule, budget, risk, and communication plans) by the Project Sponsor and key stakeholders. This confirms the project has a viable blueprint for execution.
iii. **Phase Completion Reviews (As applicable for multi-phase projects, or key delivery milestones)**: Review of deliverables and performance at significant project milestones (e.g., successful environment setup, initial component deployment). These reviews ensure deliverables are meeting requirements before proceeding to the next stage.
iv. **Change Request Approval**: Any significant changes to the project baseline (scope, schedule, budget) must go through a formal approval process, typically involving the Project Manager, Sponsor, and relevant stakeholders as defined in the Change Management Plan [Ref 1].
v.  **Final Project Acceptance (End of Closure)**: Formal acceptance of all project deliverables by the Project Sponsor or customer, signaling that the project's objectives have been met and the project can be formally closed [Ref 7].
