### Phases and Activities

The project will follow a classic lifecycle comprising initiation, planning, execution, monitoring & control, and closure, each with specific activities tailored to the marketing and education context.

#### I. Project Initiation
The goal of this phase is to define the project and secure formal authorization.
i.  **Define Project Objectives and Scope:** Clearly articulate what the project aims to achieve (e.g., increased enrollment, brand awareness), linking directly to organizational goals. A project is a "temporary endeavor to achieve one or more defined objectives" [Ref 6, Ref 13].
ii. **Identify and Engage Key Stakeholders:** Systematically identify all persons, groups, or organizations that have interests in, or can affect, or be affected by, the project [Ref 4, Ref 6]. Effective engagement with stakeholders is crucial [Ref 2, Ref 9].
iii. **Appoint Project Sponsor and Project Manager:** Establish leadership roles. The Sponsor is responsible for obtaining resources and executive decisions [Ref 6]. The Project Manager is responsible for coordinating activities to direct and control objectives [Ref 6, Ref 15].
iv. **Develop Project Charter:** Document the project's purpose, objectives, high-level requirements, scope, deliverables, and assigned project manager. This provides the project manager with the authority to apply organizational resources to project activities.
v.  **Perform Initial Risk Identification:** Identify high-level risks (threats and opportunities) that could impact the project [Ref 4, Ref 7, Ref 14].

#### II. Project Planning
This phase focuses on detailing the project scope, objectives, and developing a comprehensive plan to achieve them.
i.  **Detail Product and Project Scope:** Clearly define what the marketing campaign will deliver (product scope) and the work required to deliver it (project scope) [Ref 3]. This includes defining the target audience, messaging, channels, and desired outcomes for the education sector.
ii. **Develop Work Breakdown Structure (WBS):** Decompose the defined scope into progressively lower levels of work elements [Ref 4]. This creates a clear hierarchy of deliverables and work packages [Ref 4].
iii. **Estimate Activities, Schedule, and Budget:** Develop detailed estimates for resources, duration, and cost for each work package. This covers scheduling and budgeting as key planning elements [Ref 3].
iv. **Define Team Composition and Roles:** Identify the necessary skills and resources, and plan the project team structure [Ref 3].
v.  **Develop Management Plans:**
    *   **Communication Plan:** Define how and when information will be shared with stakeholders [Ref 3].
    *   **Risk Management Plan:** Develop strategies to optimize responses to individual and overall risks, including both opportunities and threats [Ref 14].
    *   **Quality Management Plan:** Build quality into processes and deliverables to ensure the marketing outcomes meet expectations [Ref 9].
    *   **Change Management Plan:** Establish a process for managing and controlling changes to the project scope, schedule, or budget [Ref 3].
    *   **Procurement Plan:** Identify items to be procured (e.g., media buying, creative agencies) and outline the procurement process [Ref 3].
    *   **Measurement Plan:** Define how project and marketing performance will be measured against objectives [Ref 2, Ref 3].
vi. **Baseline Project Plan:** Obtain formal approval of the comprehensive plan from the Project Sponsor and key stakeholders.

#### III. Project Execution
This phase involves carrying out the work defined in the project plan.
i.  **Direct and Manage Project Work:** Execute the planned activities, delivering the marketing campaigns and materials according to the schedule and budget [Ref 2]. Project management integrates practices to direct project execution [Ref 15].
ii. **Acquire and Develop Project Team:** Onboard and develop the project team members, fostering a collaborative environment [Ref 9]. Manage and motivate individuals involved [Ref 15].
iii. **Manage Stakeholder Engagement:** Continuously engage with stakeholders, managing their expectations and ensuring their involvement [Ref 2, Ref 9].
iv. **Conduct Procurements:** Execute procurement activities as per the procurement plan [Ref 3].
v.  **Implement Risk Responses:** Take action on agreed-upon risk response plans [Ref 14].

#### IV. Project Monitoring & Control
This phase runs concurrently with execution and focuses on tracking progress, managing changes, and ensuring the project stays on track.
i.  **Monitor Project Performance:** Track project progress against the baseline schedule, budget, and scope. Use established metrics for measurement [Ref 2, Ref 3].
ii. **Control Scope, Schedule, and Cost:** Manage variances and implement corrective actions. Address changes through the defined change management process [Ref 3].
iii. **Monitor and Control Risks:** Continuously identify new risks, analyze existing ones, and monitor the effectiveness of risk responses [Ref 14].
iv. **Manage Quality:** Ensure that project deliverables and processes adhere to the quality management plan [Ref 9].
v.  **Report Performance:** Regularly communicate project status, progress, and forecasts to stakeholders [Ref 3].

#### V. Project Closure
This final phase involves formally completing all project activities and handing over the final deliverables.
i.  **Formalize Final Deliverables:** Ensure all marketing campaigns and outputs are delivered and accepted by the client/stakeholders.
ii. **Close Procurements:** Complete all contractual agreements with suppliers and vendors.
iii. **Gain Formal Project Acceptance:** Obtain official sign-off from the Project Sponsor and key stakeholders that the project objectives have been met.
iv. **Perform Lessons Learned:** Conduct a review to identify successes, failures, and opportunities for improvement for future projects.
v.  **Release Project Team:** Formally release project resources.
vi. **Archive Project Documentation:** Ensure all project records are properly stored for future reference. PRINCE2 notes project closure as a final act [Ref 10].

### Roles

*   **Project Sponsor:** The individual responsible for obtaining the resources and making executive decisions to enable project success [Ref 6]. They provide strategic direction and champion the project within the organization.
*   **Project Manager:** The person coordinating activities to direct and control the accomplishment of agreed objectives [Ref 6, Ref 15]. Responsible for planning, executing, and closing the project.
*   **Marketing Lead:** Oversees the strategic direction and content of the marketing campaigns, ensuring alignment with organizational brand and goals.
*   **Functional Team Members (e.g., Content Creators, Designers, Digital Marketers, Data Analysts):** Individuals responsible for specific work packages [Ref 4] and activities within their respective areas of expertise.
*   **Key Stakeholders (e.g., University Leadership, Admissions, IT, Legal):** Persons, groups, or organizations that have interests in, or can affect, or be affected by, any aspect of a project [Ref 4, Ref 6]. They provide input, guidance, and validation.

### Decision Gates

Decision gates are critical points where the project's progress and viability are formally reviewed, and approval is required to proceed to the next phase. These are essential for a large, long-duration project to maintain control and ensure alignment with objectives [Ref 10].

i.  **Gate 1: Project Authorization (Post-Initiation)**
    *   **Purpose:** To confirm the project's strategic alignment, feasibility, and formal approval to proceed with detailed planning.
    *   **Inputs:** Project Charter, initial risk assessment, high-level scope, preliminary business case.
    *   **Decision:** Go/No-Go to Planning.
    *   **Evidence:** Requires Sponsor's approval, affirming the project's objectives and resource allocation [Ref 6, Ref 13].

ii. **Gate 2: Plan Approval (Post-Planning)**
    *   **Purpose:** To ensure the comprehensive project plan is complete, viable, and accepted by all key stakeholders before execution begins.
    *   **Inputs:** Detailed project plan (including scope, schedule, budget, resources, risk management, quality, communication, change, procurement, and measurement plans) [Ref 3, Ref 14].
    *   **Decision:** Approval to proceed with Execution, locking in the baseline.
    *   **Evidence:** Formal sign-off by the Project Sponsor and critical stakeholders, confirming agreement on "what the project will deliver, costs, and when" [Ref 3, Ref 5].

iii. **Gate 3: Phase Completion Review (Periodic during Execution/Monitoring & Control)**
    *   **Purpose:** For large projects, particularly those over 18 months, interim reviews are crucial at major milestone completions or every 3-6 months. This assesses cumulative progress, re-validates the business case, and approves proceeding to the next major set of activities.
    *   **Inputs:** Current project performance reports (scope, schedule, cost, quality), updated risk register, earned value analysis, stakeholder feedback.
    *   **Decision:** Continue/Adjust/Terminate for the next phase of delivery.
    *   **Evidence:** Aligns with continuous measurement [Ref 2, Ref 3] and allows for tailoring based on context and navigating complexity [Ref 9].

iv. **Gate 4: Final Acceptance (Post-Closure)**
    *   **Purpose:** To formally confirm that all project objectives have been met, deliverables accepted, and the project can be officially closed.
    *   **Inputs:** Final deliverables acceptance forms, lessons learned report, stakeholder satisfaction survey, financial closure report.
    *   **Decision:** Formal Project Closure.
    *   **Evidence:** Requires agreement on "the final act of project closure" [Ref 10] and confirmation of achieving defined objectives [Ref 6].
