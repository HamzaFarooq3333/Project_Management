#### II. Phases and Activities

The project lifecycle comprises the following phases and activities:

I.  **Initiation:** This phase defines the project's purpose and scope.
    i.   Define Project Objectives: Clearly articulate the research goals and desired outcomes.
    ii.  Identify Stakeholders: Identify individuals and groups affected by the project [Ref 9].
    iii. Develop Project Charter: Formalize project authorization and high-level objectives.
    iv. Conduct a feasibility study and define the project scope.

II. **Planning:**  A detailed plan is developed, including scope, schedule, budget, and risk management. [Ref 1]
    i.  Scope Definition: Break down the project scope into manageable work packages, creating a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) [Ref 5].
    ii.  Schedule Development: Create a realistic project schedule, outlining tasks, dependencies, and timelines.
    iii. Budgeting: Develop a project budget, considering resources and associated costs.
    iv. Risk Management Planning: Identify potential risks (threats and opportunities), assess their impact, and develop mitigation/response plans [Ref 15].
    v.  Communication Planning: Define communication channels, frequency, and reporting requirements. [Ref 1]
    vi. Quality Planning: Define quality standards and procedures.
    vii. Procurement Planning (if applicable): Plan for any necessary external resources or services.

III. **Execution:** Project work is carried out according to the plan.
    i.  Team Management: Manage and coordinate the project team.
    ii.  Resource Allocation: Allocate resources effectively.
    iii. Task Execution: Perform the research activities as outlined in the WBS.
    iv.  Communication and Collaboration: Maintain open communication with stakeholders.
    v.  Manage Risks: Implement risk response plans as needed.

IV. **Monitoring and Controlling:**  Ongoing monitoring and control of the project's progress to ensure alignment with the plan.
    i.  Progress Tracking: Monitor project performance against the schedule and budget.
    ii.  Performance Reporting: Regularly report progress to stakeholders.
    iii.  Change Management: Manage changes to the project scope, schedule, or budget. [Ref 1]
    iv.  Quality Control: Ensure deliverables meet quality standards.
    v.  Issue Management: Address and resolve project issues.

V. **Closing:** Formal project closure.
    i.  Final Deliverable Acceptance: Obtain formal acceptance of project deliverables.
    ii.  Project Documentation: Complete and archive project documentation.
    iii.  Lessons Learned: Conduct a lessons-learned session to identify improvements.
    iv.  Close Contracts: Close out any procurement contracts.

#### III. Roles

*   **Research Lead(s):** Responsible for executing research activities.
*   **Stakeholders:** Individuals and groups affected by the project [Ref 9].
*   **Team Members:** Carry out project tasks.

#### V. Decision Gates

Decision gates are crucial points where the project's progress is assessed, and go/no-go decisions are made.

I.  **Project Charter Approval:** At the end of Initiation, the Project Charter needs approval from the Project Sponsor [Ref 9].
II. **Plan Approval:** The Project Plan (including scope, schedule, budget, and risk management plans) is reviewed and approved by the Project Manager and Project Sponsor.
III. **Phase Completion Reviews:** At the end of each phase (Planning, Execution, Monitoring & Controlling), a review is conducted to ensure deliverables are met and the project is on track.
IV. **Final Deliverable Acceptance:** Formal acceptance of final deliverables by the Project Sponsor [Ref 8].
V.  **Project Closure Approval:** The Project Sponsor approves the official closure of the project upon completion of all closing activities, including lesson learned sessions [Ref 7].
